Module Overview: This module extends portfolio theory by introducing the risk-free asset, developing the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM), and distinguishing between systematic and unsystematic risk. LOS 2.a Coming Soon
Describe the implications of combining a risk-free asset with a portfolio of risky assets
Understand how adding a risk-free asset expands investment opportunities. The risk-free asset has zero variance and zero correlation with risky assets. Investors can create portfolios by combining the risk-free asset with any risky portfolio, creating a linear relationship between risk and return. This combination allows investors to achieve any desired risk level: lending (investing in risk-free asset) reduces risk below that of the risky portfolio alone, while borrowing (leveraging) at the risk-free rate increases both risk and expected return above the risky portfolio. The optimal risky portfolio is the one that, when combined with the risk-free asset, offers the best risk-return trade-off.

Explain the capital allocation line (CAL) and the capital market line (CML)
Learn the CAL and CML concepts. The Capital Allocation Line (CAL) represents all possible portfolios formed by combining the risk-free asset with a specific risky portfolio. Its slope is the Sharpe ratio of the risky portfolio. The Capital Market Line (CML) is a special CAL formed by combining the risk-free asset with the market portfolio (the tangency portfolio on the efficient frontier). LOS 2.c Coming Soon
Explain systematic and nonsystematic risk, including why an investor should not expect to receive additional return for bearing nonsystematic risk
Distinguish between two types of risk. Systematic risk (market risk, non-diversifiable risk): risk that affects all securities, arising from macroeconomic factors like interest rates, inflation, or economic cycles; cannot be eliminated through diversification; investors are compensated for bearing this risk. Nonsystematic risk (firm-specific risk, diversifiable risk): risk unique to individual securities, arising from company-specific events; can be eliminated through adequate diversification; investors receive no compensation for this risk because it can be avoided. In a well-diversified portfolio, nonsystematic risk approaches zero, leaving only systematic risk. Rational investors diversify, so only systematic risk commands a risk premium.

LOS 2.f Coming Soon
Explain the capital asset pricing model (CAPM), including its assumptions, and the security market line (SML)
Understand the CAPM framework. CAPM equation: E(R·µ¢) = Rf + Œ≤·µ¢[E(R‚Çò) - Rf], where E(R·µ¢) is expected return, Rf is risk-free rate, Œ≤·µ¢ is beta, and [E(R‚Çò) - Rf] is the market risk premium. Key assumptions: investors are risk-averse and mean-variance optimizers, markets are frictionless (no taxes or transaction costs), all investors have identical expectations, and can borrow/lend at the risk-free rate. The Security Market Line (SML) graphs the CAPM relationship, plotting expected return against beta. All properly priced assets lie on the SML. Assets above the SML are undervalued (higher return than justified by risk); assets below are overvalued.

LOS 2.g Coming Soon
Calculate and interpret the expected return of an asset using the CAPM
Apply the CAPM to determine required returns. Given the risk-free rate, market return, and asset beta, calculate expected return: E(R·µ¢) = Rf + Œ≤·µ¢[E(R‚Çò) - Rf]. For example, if Rf = 3%, E(R‚Çò) = 10%, and Œ≤ = 1.2, then E(R·µ¢) = 3% + 1.2(10% - 3%) = 11.4%. Interpretation: this is the minimum return investors require to hold the asset given its systematic risk. If actual expected return exceeds CAPM return, the asset is undervalued; if less, it's overvalued. Use CAPM for valuation (discounting cash flows), performance evaluation, and determining hurdle rates for capital budgeting.

LOS 2.h Coming Soon
Describe and demonstrate applications of the CAPM and the SML
Explore practical CAPM applications. Portfolio evaluation: compare actual returns to CAPM-required returns to assess performance. Security selection: identify mispriced securities‚Äîbuy those above the SML (higher return than required), sell those below. Capital budgeting: use CAPM to determine discount rates for projects based on their systematic risk. Performance attribution: separate returns into compensation for systematic risk versus alpha (excess return). Asset pricing: determine fair value by discounting expected cash flows at CAPM-required return. Strategic asset allocation: understand risk-return trade-offs across asset classes. The SML provides a benchmark for evaluating whether returns adequately compensate for risk taken.
