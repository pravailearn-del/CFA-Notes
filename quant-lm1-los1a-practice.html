Question 1

An investor is evaluating two investment opportunities. Investment A is a government bond yielding 5%, and Investment B is a corporate bond from a tech startup yielding 14%. The 9% difference in yields is primarily attributable to:

A) Inflation premium
B) Maturity premium
C) Default risk premium
D) Liquidity premium

✓ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: The 9% yield differential between the government bond and the tech startup bond is primarily due to the default risk premium. Government bonds are considered virtually risk-free in terms of default, while a tech startup has significantly higher default risk. Investors require this additional compensation for the possibility that the startup may fail to make promised payments. While both bonds may also differ in liquidity and maturity, the question states the difference is "primarily" attributable to one factor, and for government vs. corporate bonds, especially startups, the default risk premium is the dominant factor.

Question 2

If USD 8,000 today is equivalent to USD 9,200 in two years, the annual interest rate is closest to:

A) 7.25%
B) 7.24%
C) 15.00%
D) 6.82%

✓ Correct Answer: B

Explanation: To find the annual interest rate, we use the formula:

FV = PV × (1 + r)^n

9,200 = 8,000 × (1 + r)^2

(1 + r)^2 = 9,200 / 8,000 = 1.15

1 + r = √1.15 = 1.0724

r = 0.0724 or 7.24%

Question 3

Which of the following best describes the "opportunity cost" interpretation of an interest rate?

A) The rate used to discount future cash flows to present value
B) The minimum return required to undertake an investment
C) The return forgone by choosing one course of action over another
D) The rate that equates the present value of inflows with outflows

✓ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: The opportunity cost interpretation views the interest rate as the value forgone by choosing one course of action over another. For example, if you spend money today rather than investing it at 5%, you forgo the 5% return—that's your opportunity cost. Option A describes the discount rate interpretation, Option B describes the required rate of return interpretation, and Option D describes the internal rate of return concept.

Question 4

Consider the following two bonds:

  • Bond X: 3-year maturity, highly liquid, government-issued, 4.5% yield
  • Bond Y: 3-year maturity, thinly traded, government-issued, 5.2% yield

The 0.7% (70 basis points) difference in yields is best explained by the:

A) Default risk premium
B) Maturity premium
C) Liquidity premium
D) Inflation premium

✓ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: Both bonds have identical maturity (3 years) and both are government-issued (so same default risk). The only difference stated is that Bond X is highly liquid while Bond Y is thinly traded (illiquid). The 0.7% additional yield on Bond Y is the liquidity premium—the extra compensation investors require for holding a bond that may be difficult to sell quickly without a price concession. Thinly traded bonds have higher transaction costs and may require selling at a discount in a hurry, so investors demand higher yields to compensate.

Question 5

The real risk-free interest rate is best described as:

A) The rate on short-term government securities minus expected inflation
B) The interest rate that would exist on a risk-free security if no inflation were expected
C) The rate on long-term government bonds
D) The nominal interest rate on Treasury bills

✓ Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The real risk-free interest rate is defined as the single-period interest rate for a completely risk-free security if no inflation were expected. It reflects the time preferences of individuals for current versus future real consumption—essentially what people demand to defer consumption even in a world with no risk and no inflation. Option A describes how we might estimate it empirically, but it's not the definition. Options C and D describe nominal rates that include inflation premiums.

Question 6

If the real risk-free rate is 2.5%, expected inflation is 3.5%, default risk premium is 1.5%, liquidity premium is 0.5%, and maturity premium is 1.0%, the appropriate interest rate is:

A) 7.5%
B) 8.0%
C) 9.0%
D) 9.5%

✓ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: The interest rate is the sum of all components:

r = Real risk-free rate + Inflation premium + Default risk premium + Liquidity premium + Maturity premium

r = 2.5% + 3.5% + 1.5% + 0.5% + 1.0%

r = 9.0%

Question 7

An investor has INR 500,000 and can either invest in a fixed deposit yielding 7% or start a small business with expected returns of 18%. If the investor chooses to start the business, the opportunity cost is:

A) 7%
B) 11%
C) 18%
D) 25%

✓ Correct Answer: A

Explanation: The opportunity cost is what you give up by choosing one option over another. By choosing to start the business, the investor gives up the 7% return from the fixed deposit. That forgone 7% return is the opportunity cost of starting the business. The 18% is the expected return from the chosen option (the business), not the opportunity cost. The 11% difference is not the opportunity cost—it's the incremental expected return for taking on the additional risk.

Question 8

Which of the following statements about the nominal risk-free rate is most accurate?

A) It equals the real risk-free rate plus all risk premiums
B) It can be approximated as the sum of the real risk-free rate and the inflation premium
C) It includes compensation for default risk
D) It is always greater than corporate bond yields

✓ Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The nominal risk-free rate is the combination of the real risk-free rate and the inflation premium. More precisely: (1 + nominal risk-free rate) = (1 + real risk-free rate) × (1 + inflation premium). In practice, this is often approximated as: Nominal risk-free rate ≈ Real risk-free rate + Inflation premium. Option A is incorrect because the nominal risk-free rate does not include risk premiums like default, liquidity, or maturity premiums. Option C is incorrect because "risk-free" means no default risk. Option D is incorrect because the comparison depends on the specific securities and market conditions.

Question 9

Holding all other factors constant, which bond would you expect to have the highest yield?

A) 10-year US Treasury bond
B) 10-year high-quality corporate bond, actively traded
C) 10-year low-quality corporate bond, thinly traded
D) 2-year US Treasury bond

✓ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: Bond C would have the highest yield because it has both high default risk (low-quality) and low liquidity (thinly traded). Comparing all options:

  • Option A: No default risk, high liquidity, 10-year maturity
  • Option B: Some default risk, high liquidity, 10-year maturity
  • Option C: High default risk + low liquidity + 10-year maturity = Highest yield
  • Option D: No default risk, high liquidity, but shorter maturity

Bond C must compensate investors with the highest yield due to its combination of risks.

Question 10

All else equal, if market participants' inflation expectations increase, the most likely effect on interest rates would be:

A) Interest rates would decrease
B) Interest rates would remain unchanged
C) Interest rates would increase
D) The real risk-free rate would increase

✓ Correct Answer: C

Explanation: If inflation expectations increase, investors will demand higher nominal interest rates to maintain their real returns. The inflation premium is a component of interest rates, so if expected inflation rises, interest rates will increase accordingly. For example, if the real risk-free rate is 2% and expected inflation rises from 3% to 5%, the nominal risk-free rate would rise from approximately 5% to 7%. Option D is incorrect because the real risk-free rate is independent of inflation expectations—it reflects time preferences for consumption, not inflation.
