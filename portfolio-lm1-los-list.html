Portfolio Risk and Return: Part I

Module Overview: This module introduces the foundational concepts of modern portfolio theory, covering major asset classes, risk-return relationships, and portfolio diversification. You'll learn how to measure portfolio risk, understand the benefits of combining assets with less-than-perfect correlation, and explore the efficient frontier concept.

Describe characteristics of the major asset classes that investors consider in forming portfolios
Understand the key features of major asset classes: equities (ownership, capital appreciation, dividends, higher risk/return), fixed income (contractual cash flows, lower risk than equities, interest rate sensitivity), real estate (tangible assets, income generation, inflation hedge), commodities (physical goods, inflation protection, no cash flows), and alternative investments (private equity, hedge funds, low correlation). Learn how each asset class differs in terms of expected return, risk characteristics, liquidity, and role in portfolio diversification.

LOS 1.b Coming Soon
Explain risk aversion and its implications for portfolio selection
Understand risk aversion as the tendency for investors to prefer certainty over uncertainty and to require compensation (higher expected return) for taking additional risk. Learn how risk aversion affects portfolio choice: risk-averse investors seek the highest expected return for a given level of risk, or the lowest risk for a given expected return. Explore utility functions that reflect investor preferences, where utility increases with return but decreases with risk, leading investors to make trade-offs between risk and return based on their degree of risk aversion.

LOS 1.c Coming Soon
Explain the selection of an optimal portfolio, given an investor's utility (or risk aversion) and the capital allocation line
Learn how investors select optimal portfolios where their indifference curve (representing constant utility) is tangent to the capital allocation line (CAL). The CAL represents all possible combinations of the risk-free asset and a risky portfolio. The optimal portfolio maximizes utility for a given level of risk aversion‚Äîmore risk-averse investors allocate more to the risk-free asset, while less risk-averse investors allocate more to the risky portfolio. This tangency point represents the highest achievable utility given the investment opportunities available.

LOS 1.d Coming Soon
Calculate and interpret the mean, variance, and covariance (or correlation) of asset returns based on historical data
Master the calculation of key statistical measures. Mean return: average of historical returns, representing expected return. Variance: average squared deviation from the mean, measuring dispersion of returns. Standard deviation: square root of variance, expressing risk in the same units as returns. Covariance: measure of how two assets move together; positive covariance means they tend to move in the same direction. Correlation: standardized covariance ranging from -1 to +1, where +1 means perfect positive correlation, -1 means perfect negative correlation, and 0 means no linear relationship. LOS 1.f Coming Soon
Describe the effect on a portfolio's risk of investing in assets that are less than perfectly correlated
Understand the power of diversification through combining assets with less-than-perfect positive correlation. When correlation is less than +1, portfolio risk is reduced below the weighted average of individual asset risks. The diversification benefit is greatest when assets are negatively correlated (correlation < 0) or uncorrelated (correlation = 0). With perfect positive correlation (+1), no diversification benefit exists. With perfect negative correlation (-1), it's theoretically possible to eliminate risk entirely. This principle explains why diversification across asset classes, sectors, and geographies can significantly reduce portfolio volatility while maintaining or enhancing expected returns.

LOS 1.g Coming Soon
Describe and interpret the minimum-variance and efficient frontiers of risky assets and the global minimum-variance portfolio
Explore the efficient frontier, which represents the set of portfolios offering the highest expected return for each level of risk, or the lowest risk for each level of expected return. The minimum-variance frontier shows all portfolios with the minimum variance for a given return level. The global minimum-variance portfolio is the single portfolio on the minimum-variance frontier with the lowest possible risk. The efficient frontier consists of portfolios on the minimum-variance frontier that lie above the global minimum-variance portfolio‚Äîthese are the only portfolios a rational investor would consider. All portfolios below the efficient frontier are suboptimal because higher returns are achievable at the same risk level.
