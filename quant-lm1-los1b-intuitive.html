üéØ What You'll Learn

Understanding how to measure investment returns is crucial for comparing investments and evaluating performance. This lesson covers three fundamental ways to calculate returns: holding period returns, arithmetic mean returns, and geometric mean returns. Each serves a different purpose and tells a different story about your investments.

üí° The Big Picture

Think of returns like measuring a journey. You can measure:

  • The single trip - How did I do from start to finish? (Holding Period Return)
  • The simple average of all trips - What's my typical performance? (Arithmetic Mean)
  • The compound growth rate - What consistent rate would get me the same result? (Geometric Mean)

üé¨ Understanding Returns: The Two Sources

Financial assets generate returns in two ways, much like owning rental property:

üè† The Real Estate Analogy

Income Return (Like Rental Income)

Just as a rental property generates monthly rent, stocks pay dividends and bonds pay interest. This is cash you receive while holding the asset.

Capital Gain/Loss (Like Property Value Change)

Just as your property might increase or decrease in value, your stock price can go up or down. You realize this gain or loss when you sell.

Example: You buy a house for $100,000. It generates $500/month in rent ($6,000/year) and after a year is worth $105,000.

  • Income Return: $6,000 √∑ $100,000 = 6%
  • Capital Gain: $5,000 √∑ $100,000 = 5%
  • Total Return: 6% + 5% = 11%

This is the simplest return measure - what did you earn over the entire time you held the investment?

üßÆ Method 2: Arithmetic Mean Return

The arithmetic mean is the simple average of all returns. It's what most people think of when they hear "average."

üçï The Pizza Analogy

Imagine tracking how many slices of pizza you eat each day for a week: 2, 3, 2, 4, 2, 3, 5 slices.

Arithmetic Mean: (2+3+2+4+2+3+5) √∑ 7 = 3 slices per day

It's just the total divided by the number of days - simple and straightforward!

Annual Returns: -50%, +35%, +27%

Arithmetic Mean:

Interpretation: On average, the fund returned 4% per year. But here's the catch - this doesn't tell you what actually happened to your money!

üìà Method 3: Geometric Mean Return

The geometric mean accounts for compounding. It answers: "What consistent annual return would give me the same ending value?"

üå± The Plant Growth Analogy

Imagine a plant that:

  • Doubles in height in Year 1 (100% growth)
  • Halves in height in Year 2 (-50% growth)

Arithmetic Mean: (100% - 50%) / 2 = 25% per year

But wait! The plant is back to its original height! The actual growth rate is 0%, not 25%.

The geometric mean correctly shows zero growth!

üéØ Critical Insight: The arithmetic mean is always higher than the geometric mean (unless all returns are equal). The bigger the variation in returns, especially with negative returns, the larger the gap between the two means. The geometric mean is the accurate measure of what actually happened to your investment.

üìä When to Use Which Mean?

Return Measure Use When... Best For...
Arithmetic Mean You need a simple average or want to forecast expected returns Statistical analysis, forward-looking estimates
Geometric Mean You want to know what actually happened to an investment over time Historical performance, comparing investments, portfolio reporting
Holding Period Return You need the total return over a specific, single period Short-term performance measurement

üéì Key Takeaways

‚úÖ Remember These Points

  • Holding Period Return: The total return over any single time period
  • Arithmetic Mean: Simple average - good for forecasting but overstates actual growth
  • Geometric Mean: Compound average - accurately reflects what happened to your money
  • Compounding Matters: Multi-year returns must be compounded, not simply added
  • Arithmetic > Geometric: The arithmetic mean is always higher unless all returns are identical
  • Volatility Penalty: The more volatile the returns, the bigger the gap between arithmetic and geometric means
üéØ Exam Tip: In exam questions, pay close attention to whether they ask for "average return" (arithmetic) or "compound annual growth rate" or "actual growth" (geometric). The question will guide you to the right method!
