Understanding Interest Rates: The Feynman Way

ü§î What Really Is an Interest Rate?

Imagine you have ‚Çπ10,000 today. Would you rather have this money now or wait a year to receive it? Most people would choose to have it now. Why? Because money today is more valuable than money tomorrow.

Interest rate is simply the "rent" you charge for letting someone use your money. Just like you pay rent to live in someone's house, borrowers pay interest to use your money.

Think of it this way: If you lend ‚Çπ9,500 today and get back ‚Çπ10,000 in one year, the extra ‚Çπ500 is your "rent" for letting someone use your money. That's 5.26% interest (500 √∑ 9,500 = 0.0526 or 5.26%).

Three Ways to Think About Interest Rates

üé≠ The Three Faces of Interest Rates

Interest rates wear three different hats depending on who's looking at them:

1Ô∏è‚É£ Required Rate of Return - The Investor's Perspective

Think of it like dating: You have certain minimum standards. Similarly, investors have a minimum return they'll accept to invest their money.

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example:

Rajesh has ‚Çπ1 lakh to invest. He can:

  • Put it in an SBI Fixed Deposit at 6.5% (safe)
  • Buy Infosys shares (riskier, but potential for higher returns)
  • Invest in a startup (very risky)

For Rajesh to invest in Infosys, he would require at least 12-15% return because it's riskier than FD. For a startup, he might require 25-30% because it's extremely risky. This minimum acceptable return is the required rate of return.

üåç Global Example:

Warren Buffett famously says he won't invest unless he can expect at least a 15% annual return. That's his "hurdle rate" or required rate of return. If a business can't clear that hurdle, he won't invest, no matter how good it looks.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Discount Rate - The "What's It Worth Today?" Perspective

Imagine winning a lottery: Would you rather get ‚Çπ1 crore today or ‚Çπ1.1 crore next year? The discount rate helps you compare these options.

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example:

Priya is buying a flat in Mumbai. The builder offers two payment options:

  • Pay ‚Çπ1 crore today
  • Pay ‚Çπ1.08 crore after one year

If Priya can earn 10% by investing in mutual funds, the ‚Çπ1.08 crore next year is worth only ‚Çπ98.2 lakhs today (1.08 crore √∑ 1.10). So she should pay ‚Çπ1 crore today instead! The 10% is the discount rate that converts future money to present value.

üåç Global Example:

When Amazon decides whether to build a new warehouse, they calculate: "This warehouse will generate $50 million over 10 years. What's that worth today?" Using a 12% discount rate, they can figure out if spending $30 million today makes sense.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Opportunity Cost - The "What Am I Giving Up?" Perspective

Life is full of trade-offs: Choosing one thing means saying no to another. The opportunity cost is what you're giving up.

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example:

Anil has ‚Çπ5 lakhs. He can either:

  • Buy a new car (consume now)
  • Invest in PPF at 7.1% (save and earn)

If he buys the car, his opportunity cost is the 7.1% return he's giving up. In 15 years, that ‚Çπ5 lakhs would have become ‚Çπ14 lakhs! So the real cost of the car isn't just ‚Çπ5 lakhs‚Äîit's ‚Çπ14 lakhs of future wealth.

üåç Global Example:

Mark Zuckerberg was offered $1 billion for Facebook in 2006. He said no. His opportunity cost? $1 billion in cash today. But Facebook is now worth over $800 billion. He made the right call on opportunity cost!

What Makes Up an Interest Rate?

üèóÔ∏è The Five Building Blocks

1. Real Risk-Free Rate - The Foundation

Analogy: This is like the base price of a pizza before any toppings. It's what you'd charge even in a perfect world with no risks.

What it means: This reflects people's basic preference for money today vs. money tomorrow, even if there's zero risk and zero inflation. Historically, this has been around 2-3% globally.

üáÆüá≥ Example: Even if India had zero inflation and zero risk, you'd still want some return for waiting. That's the real risk-free rate.

2. Inflation Premium - Protection Against Rising Prices

Analogy: Remember when your parents said "‚Çπ100 used to buy so much more in our time"? That's inflation eating away purchasing power.

What it means: If inflation is 6%, you need to earn at least 6% just to maintain your purchasing power.

üáÆüá≥ Example: In 2010, a movie ticket in Mumbai cost ‚Çπ150. Today it's ‚Çπ350. If you had ‚Çπ150 under your mattress since 2010, you can't even buy a ticket today! The inflation premium compensates for this.

üåç Example: Venezuela had 1,000,000% inflation in 2018. If you lent money there, you'd need a huge inflation premium, or your money would be worthless when you got it back!

3. Default Risk Premium - The "Will I Get My Money Back?" Fee

Analogy: Would you lend ‚Çπ1000 to your best friend or to a stranger on the street at the same interest rate? Of course not! The stranger needs to pay you more because they might not pay you back.

What it means: Riskier borrowers must pay higher interest rates.

üáÆüá≥ Example:

  • Indian Government 10-year bond: ~7% (very safe)
  • Tata Sons bond: ~8% (safe, but not as safe as government)
  • Small company bond: ~12% (riskier)

That extra 1% and 5% is the default risk premium!

üåç Example: Greek government bonds paid 30%+ interest during their debt crisis because investors feared Greece might default. Meanwhile, German bonds paid only 2% because Germany is super reliable.

4. Liquidity Premium - The "Can I Sell This Quickly?" Fee

Analogy: Imagine you own two things worth ‚Çπ10 lakhs: gold coins vs. a painting. Which can you sell faster without losing value? Gold coins! The painting needs time to find the right buyer.

What it means: Investments that are hard to sell quickly must offer higher returns.

üáÆüá≥ Example: SBI shares trade millions of times daily on NSE‚Äîsuper liquid. But try selling shares of a small company listed on BSE‚Äîit might take days or weeks to find a buyer, and you might have to sell at a discount. That's why illiquid stocks need to offer higher returns.

üåç Example: During the 2008 financial crisis, even good mortgage bonds became illiquid. Nobody wanted to buy them. Sellers had to offer huge discounts (meaning buyers got huge liquidity premiums).

5. Maturity Premium - The "How Long Do I Have to Wait?" Fee

Analogy: Would you rather lend your friend ‚Çπ10,000 for 1 month or 10 years? The longer period feels riskier and more uncertain, right? That's why longer loans need higher interest.

What it means: Longer-term investments usually offer higher returns because a lot can change over time.

üáÆüá≥ Example:

  • 91-day Treasury Bill: 6.5%
  • 5-year Government Bond: 7.2%
  • 10-year Government Bond: 7.4%

Notice how longer maturity = higher rate? That extra return is the maturity premium.

üåç Example: US 3-month T-bills might yield 5%, while 30-year bonds yield 6%. That 1% difference is mostly the maturity premium.

Putting It All Together

üßÆ The Interest Rate Formula

Interest Rate (r) = Real Risk-Free Rate + Inflation Premium + Default Risk Premium + Liquidity Premium + Maturity Premium

üìä Real Example: Building an Interest Rate

Let's calculate the interest rate for a 10-year corporate bond from a mid-sized Indian company:

Component Rate Reasoning
Real Risk-Free Rate 2.5% Basic time preference for money
Inflation Premium 5.0% Expected average inflation in India
Default Risk Premium 2.0% Mid-sized company, moderate risk
Liquidity Premium 0.5% Not very liquid in secondary market
Maturity Premium 1.0% 10-year maturity has interest rate risk
Total Interest Rate 11.0% Sum of all components

So this company would need to offer approximately 11% interest on its 10-year bonds!

üí° Key Insight: Interest rates constantly change because all these components are constantly moving:
  • RBI changes rates ‚Üí affects real risk-free rate
  • Oil prices rise ‚Üí inflation expectations change
  • Company announces losses ‚Üí default risk premium increases
  • Market crash ‚Üí liquidity premium spikes as everyone panics
This is why interest rates in the market fluctuate every single day!
