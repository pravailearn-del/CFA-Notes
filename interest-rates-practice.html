Question 1 Easy
An investor lends ‚Çπ95,000 today and expects to receive ‚Çπ100,000 one year from now. The interest rate on this loan is closest to:
A 5.00%
B 5.26%
C 4.76%

‚úì Correct Answer: B (5.26%)

Calculation:

Interest rate = (Future Value - Present Value) / Present Value

r = (‚Çπ100,000 - ‚Çπ95,000) / ‚Çπ95,000

r = ‚Çπ5,000 / ‚Çπ95,000

r = 0.0526 or 5.26%

Explanation: The investor requires 5.26% compensation to lend ‚Çπ95,000 for one year. This represents the time value of money - the rate that makes the investor indifferent between ‚Çπ95,000 today and ‚Çπ100,000 in one year.

Question 2 Easy
Which of the following statements about interest rates is most accurate?
A Interest rates can only be interpreted as required rates of return.
B Interest rates serve as discount rates, required returns, and opportunity costs.
C The terms "interest rate" and "discount rate" should never be used interchangeably.

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Explanation: Interest rates have three conceptual interpretations:

  • Required Rate of Return: The minimum return needed to make an investment worthwhile
  • Discount Rate: The rate used to convert future cash flows to present value
  • Opportunity Cost: The return forgone by choosing one investment over another

All three perspectives refer to the same numerical rate but emphasize different aspects of its role in financial decision-making.

Why C is incorrect: The terms "interest rate" and "discount rate" are commonly used interchangeably in practice, particularly in present value calculations.

Question 3 Medium

A 5.50%
B 5.58%

(1 + Nominal rate) = (1 + Real rate)(1 + Inflation rate)

(1 + Nominal) = (1.025)(1.030)

(1 + Nominal) = 1.05575

Question 4 Easy
Which component of an interest rate compensates investors for the loss of purchasing power over time?
A Real risk-free rate
B Inflation premium
C Liquidity premium

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The inflation premium compensates investors for the expected decline in purchasing power due to inflation.

Example: If inflation is 4% per year, a basket of goods costing ‚Çπ100 today will cost ‚Çπ104 next year. Without an inflation premium, an investor receiving ‚Çπ100 in the future would be able to buy 4% less than they could today.

Why A is incorrect: The real risk-free rate reflects time preferences, not inflation compensation.

Why C is incorrect: The liquidity premium compensates for the difficulty of converting an investment to cash, not purchasing power loss.

Question 5 Medium
Two bonds have identical characteristics except that Bond A trades frequently with high volume, while Bond B rarely trades. If Bond A yields 4.5% and Bond B yields 5.2%, the liquidity premium is closest to:
A 0.5%
B 0.7%
C 1.0%

‚úì Correct Answer: B (0.7%)

Calculation:

Liquidity Premium = Yield on less liquid bond - Yield on more liquid bond

Liquidity Premium = 5.2% - 4.5% = 0.7%

Explanation: Since the bonds are identical except for liquidity, the 0.7% (70 basis points) yield difference represents the compensation investors require for holding the less liquid Bond B. This premium reflects:

  • Higher transaction costs when selling
  • Wider bid-ask spreads
  • Greater price impact from selling
  • Potential difficulty finding buyers

All three bonds are government bonds (no default risk or liquidity premium differences). Since the inflation premium is constant across maturities, the yield differences are due solely to maturity premiums.

Question 7 Easy
Which of the following statements about the real risk-free interest rate is most accurate?
A It includes compensation for expected inflation.
B It reflects individuals' time preferences for current versus future consumption.
C It is directly observable in market prices of Treasury securities.

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The real risk-free rate is the theoretical interest rate on a completely risk-free security assuming zero inflation. It reflects the fundamental preference of individuals to consume now rather than later - the "time preference" for consumption.

Why A is incorrect: The real risk-free rate specifically excludes inflation. When you add an inflation premium to the real risk-free rate, you get the nominal risk-free rate.

Why C is incorrect: The real risk-free rate is not directly observable. Market prices reflect nominal rates (which include inflation). The real rate must be inferred by subtracting expected inflation from observed nominal rates.

Question 8 Medium
A corporate bond and a government bond both have 5-year maturities and are highly liquid. The corporate bond yields 6.5% while the government bond yields 4.0%. The difference in yields is most likely attributable to:
A Liquidity premium
B Default risk premium
C Maturity premium

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Analysis:

  • Same maturity (5 years): No maturity premium difference
  • Both highly liquid: No significant liquidity premium difference
  • Corporate vs. Government: Government bonds have virtually no default risk

Conclusion: The 2.5% yield difference (6.5% - 4.0%) represents the default risk premium. This compensates investors for the possibility that the corporate issuer might fail to make scheduled payments.

Credit Spread: The difference between corporate and government bond yields is commonly called the "credit spread" and directly measures the default risk premium.

Question 10 Medium
An investor can spend $10,000 today on consumption or invest it in a bond yielding 6% annually. By investing instead of consuming, the investor's opportunity cost is:
A The satisfaction from $10,000 of current consumption.
B 6% per year.
C $600 of interest income.

‚úì Correct Answer: A

Explanation: When the investor chooses to invest rather than consume, the opportunity cost is what they give up - the satisfaction (utility) from consuming $10,000 today.

Understanding Opportunity Cost:

If the investor chose to consume instead of invest, their opportunity cost would be the 6% return (or $600) they could have earned. But since they're investing, the opportunity cost is the current consumption forgone.

Why B and C are incorrect: These represent what the investor GAINS by investing, not what they FORGO. The 6% return is compensation for bearing the opportunity cost, not the opportunity cost itself.

Question 11 Hard
An analyst constructs the following interest rate for a 5-year corporate bond:
  • Real risk-free rate: 1.5%
  • Inflation premium: 2.8%
  • Default risk premium: 1.8%
  • Liquidity premium: 0.6%
  • Maturity premium: 0.9%
If the analyst observes that this bond is actually trading at a yield of 8.5%, which explanation is most consistent with this observation?
A The bond is overvalued and should be sold.
B The market perceives higher default risk than the analyst estimated.
C The market expects lower inflation than the analyst estimated.

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Analyst's Calculated Yield:

1.5% + 2.8% + 1.8% + 0.6% + 0.9% = 7.6%

Actual Market Yield: 8.5%

Difference: 8.5% - 7.6% = 0.9%

Analysis: The market is demanding a higher yield (lower price) than the analyst's model suggests. This indicates the market perceives more risk than the analyst estimated. The most likely explanation is higher default risk, as this premium can vary significantly based on company-specific factors and market sentiment.

Why A is incorrect: If the bond were overvalued, it would have a lower yield than calculated, not higher.

Why C is incorrect: Lower expected inflation would result in a lower required yield, not higher.

Question 12 Medium
Which of the following best explains why a 10-year US Treasury bond typically has a higher yield than a 3-month US Treasury bill?
A The 10-year bond has higher default risk.
B The 10-year bond has greater interest rate sensitivity and uncertainty.
C The 10-year bond has lower liquidity.

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Explanation: Both securities are US Treasury obligations, so they have:

  • Essentially zero default risk (backed by US government)
  • High liquidity (both trade in deep, active markets)

The yield difference is primarily due to the maturity premium. The 10-year bond has:

  • Greater interest rate risk: Its price is more sensitive to changes in market interest rates
  • More uncertainty: Economic conditions, inflation, and monetary policy over 10 years are harder to predict than over 3 months
  • Longer opportunity cost: Capital is tied up for a longer period

Investors demand a premium (higher yield) to compensate for these factors, creating the upward-sloping yield curve typically observed for Treasury securities.
