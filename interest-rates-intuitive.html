Let's Break Down Each Layer ğŸ“š

ğŸŸ¢ Layer 1: Real Risk-Free Rate (The Foundation)

What it is: This is the most basic return - what you'd earn if there was zero risk and zero inflation.

Think of it as: The price people put on "having money now vs. later." Even if everything was perfectly safe and prices never changed, would you still want some compensation for waiting? That's this rate!

Real-world example: If people are generally impatient (want money now), this rate is higher. If people are patient (happy to wait), it's lower.

ğŸŸ  Layer 2: Inflation Premium

What it is: Compensation for the fact that money loses purchasing power over time.

Think of it as: Today, â‚¹100 buys you 10 samosas. But next year, those same 10 samosas might cost â‚¹106. You need extra money just to keep up with rising prices!

ğŸ”´ Layer 3: Default Risk Premium

What it is: Extra return you demand because the borrower might not pay you back.

Think of it as: Would you lend money to your reliable friend at the same rate as a stranger you met yesterday? Probably not! The stranger might disappear!

ğŸŒ Global Example: Government vs. Corporate Bonds

Two bonds, both for 1 year:

Bond Type Interest Rate Default Risk
US Treasury Bond 3.0% Almost Zero (government backs it)

The 5% difference? That's the default risk premium! The startup has to offer 5% extra to convince you to take the risk that they might not pay.

ğŸ”µ Layer 4: Liquidity Premium

What it is: Extra return for investing in something you can't easily sell without losing money.

Think of it as: It's like the difference between selling gold (easy, many buyers) vs. selling your used furniture (harder, fewer buyers, might need to discount).

  • Hard to find buyers if you need to sell early
  • Might have to sell at a discount - low liquidity

That 2.4% extra? Part of it is liquidity premium - compensation for being stuck with an investment that's hard to sell!

ğŸŸ£ Layer 5: Maturity Premium

What it is: Extra return for tying up your money for a longer period.

Think of it as: Would you rather lock your money away for 1 year or 10 years? Most people prefer shorter periods because a lot can change! Longer investments are riskier (interest rates might rise, you might need the money), so they pay more.
