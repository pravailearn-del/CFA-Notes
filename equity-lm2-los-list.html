Security Market Indices

Module Overview: This module covers the construction, calculation, and uses of security market indices. Learn about different weighting methods, index management, and how indices serve as benchmarks for portfolio performance.

by Paul D. Kaplan, PhD, CFA, and Dorothy C. Kelly, CFA. Paul D. Kaplan, PhD, CFA, is at Morningstar (Canada). Dorothy C. Kelly, CFA, is at McIntire School of Commerce, University of Virginia (USA).

Learning Outcome Statements (LOS)

LOS 2.a Coming Soon
Describe a security market index
Understand that a security market index represents a specific market or market segment through a portfolio of securities. Learn how indices serve multiple purposes: measuring market performance, serving as benchmarks for investment performance, providing the basis for index funds and ETFs, and reflecting investor sentiment and economic conditions.

LOS 2.b Coming Soon
Calculate and interpret the value, price return, and total return of an index
Master the calculations for index value (current portfolio value relative to base value), price return (percentage change in index value excluding income), and total return (including dividends and other income). Understand the difference between price return indices and total return indices and when each is appropriate for performance measurement.

LOS 2.c Coming Soon
Describe the choices and issues in index construction and management
Learn about key decisions in index construction: target market selection (what securities to include), security selection criteria (size, liquidity, sector), weighting method (price-weighted, equal-weighted, market-cap-weighted), rebalancing frequency, and reconstitution procedures. Understand trade-offs between broad representation and investability.

LOS 2.d Coming Soon
Compare the different weighting methods used in index construction
Compare price-weighted indices (sum of prices divided by divisor, sensitive to stock splits), equal-weighted indices (equal amounts invested in each security, highest turnover), market-capitalization-weighted indices (weighted by market value, most common), and fundamental-weighted indices (weighted by financial metrics like revenue or dividends). Analyze advantages and disadvantages of each method.

LOS 2.e Coming Soon
Calculate and analyze the value and return of an index given its weighting method
Practice calculating index values and returns for different weighting methods. For price-weighted indices, compute the divisor and index value. For market-cap-weighted indices, calculate weights based on market value. For equal-weighted indices, determine required rebalancing trades. Understand how different weighting schemes affect index performance and composition over time.

LOS 2.f Coming Soon
Describe rebalancing and reconstitution of an index
Understand rebalancing (adjusting weights back to target levels, particularly for equal-weighted or fixed-weight indices) versus reconstitution (changing index membership by adding/removing securities). Learn about the frequency, methodology, and impact of each process. Analyze how index changes can create price pressure and trading opportunities.

LOS 2.g Coming Soon
Describe uses of security market indexes
Learn the major uses of indices: gauging market sentiment and economic trends, benchmarking portfolio performance, creating index-tracking portfolios (passive investing), serving as underlying assets for derivatives and structured products, measuring market risk exposures (betas), and allocating assets across market segments. Understand how each application requires specific index characteristics.

Describe types of equity indexes

Compare types of security market indexes
Compare equity indices with fixed-income indices (challenges of bond index construction due to diverse issuance, illiquidity), commodity indices (futures-based, storage costs), real estate indices (appraisal-based vs. transaction-based), and hedge fund indices (survivorship bias, self-selection bias). LOS 2.j Coming Soon
Describe types of fixed-income indexes
Learn about fixed-income index types: sovereign bond indices, corporate bond indices (investment grade vs. high yield), municipal bond indices, and aggregate bond indices. Understand unique challenges in bond indexing: large universe of securities, illiquid trading, bonds maturing and being replaced, and the difficulty of replication due to the sheer number of issues.
