1Ô∏è‚É£ Understanding Interest Rates

üéØ Learning Objective: Interpret interest rates as required rates of return, discount rates, or opportunity costs and explain an interest rate as the sum of a real risk-free rate and premiums that compensate investors for bearing distinct types of risk.

üí° What is an Interest Rate?

An interest rate reflects the relationship between cash flows occurring at different times. It's the compensation required for receiving money in the future rather than today.

Example: If USD 9,500 today equals USD 10,000 in one year, the interest rate is:
(10,000 - 9,500) / 9,500 = 5.26%

üìà Required Rate of Return

Minimum rate an investor must receive to accept an investment

üí∞ Discount Rate

Rate at which future cash flows are equivalent to present value

üîÑ Opportunity Cost

Value forgone by choosing one course of action over another

üß© Components of Interest Rates

Interest Rate Formula:
r = Real Risk-Free Rate + Inflation Premium + Default Risk Premium + Liquidity Premium + Maturity Premium
üè¶ Real Risk-Free Interest Rate: The single-period rate for a completely risk-free security with no expected inflation. Reflects time preferences for current vs. future consumption.
üìä Inflation Premium: Compensates for expected inflation over the debt's maturity. Inflation reduces purchasing power of currency.
‚ö†Ô∏è Default Risk Premium: Compensates for the possibility that the borrower fails to make promised payments on time and in full.
üíß Liquidity Premium: Compensates for risk of loss if investment must be converted to cash quickly. T-bills have NO liquidity premium; small issuer bonds DO.
‚è≥ Maturity Premium: Compensates for increased price sensitivity as maturity extends. Longer-term debt typically has higher maturity premium.
üîë Nominal Risk-Free Rate:
(1 + Nominal Risk-Free Rate) = (1 + Real Risk-Free Rate) √ó (1 + Inflation Premium)

Approximation: Nominal Risk-Free Rate ‚âà Real Risk-Free Rate + Inflation Premium
