Detailed Notes

Interest Rates and Time Value of Money

Learning Outcome Statement

Interpret interest rates as required rates of return, discount rates, or opportunity costs and explain an interest rate as the sum of a real risk-free rate and premiums that compensate investors for bearing distinct types of risk.

1. Foundational Concept: Time Value of Money

The time value of money principle establishes that cash flows occurring at different points in time are not directly comparable. This fundamental concept underpins all financial analysis and valuation.

Core Principle

A dollar received today is worth more than a dollar promised in the future. This preference for current consumption over future consumption necessitates compensation for deferring consumption - this compensation is the interest rate.

1.1 Mathematical Foundation

An interest rate (or yield), denoted as r, represents the rate of return that establishes equivalence between cash flows at different points in time. The basic relationship can be expressed as:

Future Value = Present Value × (1 + r)

Or rearranged:

r = (Future Value - Present Value) / Present Value

Example 1: Basic Interest Rate Calculation

Given:

  • Present Value (PV) = USD 9,500
  • Future Value (FV) = USD 10,000 (one year from now)

Required compensation:

FV - PV = USD 10,000 - USD 9,500 = USD 500

Interest rate:

r = USD 500 / USD 9,500 = 0.0526 or 5.26%

Interpretation: An investor requires a 5.26% return to be indifferent between receiving USD 9,500 today and USD 10,000 in one year.

2. Three Interpretations of Interest Rates

Interest rates serve multiple conceptual roles in financial analysis. Understanding these perspectives is essential for proper application in different contexts.

2.1 Required Rate of Return

Definition: The minimum rate of return an investor must receive to accept the investment opportunity.

Application: Used in investment decision-making and asset valuation. If an investment's expected return falls below the required rate, the investment should be rejected.

2.2 Discount Rate

Definition: The rate used to convert future cash flows to their present value equivalent.

Application: Used in present value calculations and discounted cash flow (DCF) analysis. The terms "interest rate" and "discount rate" are often used interchangeably in practice.

The present value relationship is expressed as:

PV = FV / (1 + r)^n

Where:

  • PV = Present Value
  • FV = Future Value
  • r = Discount rate (interest rate)
  • n = Number of periods

2.3 Opportunity Cost

Definition: The value forgone by choosing one course of action over the next best alternative.

Application: In the context of interest rates, it represents the return forgone by consuming rather than investing, or by choosing one investment over another.

Example 2: Three Perspectives on the Same Rate

An investor evaluates a corporate bond offering 8% annual return.

As Required Return:

"Given the bond's risk characteristics, I require at least 8% to justify this investment."

As Discount Rate:

"I will discount the bond's future cash flows at 8% to determine its present value."

As Opportunity Cost:

"By investing in this bond, I forgo the opportunity to earn returns elsewhere; my opportunity cost is 8%."

3. Components of Interest Rates

Market-determined interest rates reflect multiple economic factors and risk considerations. The fundamental decomposition is:

r = Real risk-free interest rate

+ Inflation premium

+ Default risk premium

+ Liquidity premium

+ Maturity premium

3.1 Real Risk-Free Interest Rate

Definition: The single-period interest rate for a completely risk-free security assuming no expected inflation.

Economic Basis: Reflects individuals' time preferences for current versus future real consumption. Determined by the supply of and demand for savings in an economy.

Determinants:

  • Economic growth expectations (higher growth → higher real rate)
  • Demographic factors (aging populations may increase savings supply)
  • Government fiscal policy (deficits can increase demand for funds)
  • Productivity trends

3.2 Inflation Premium

Definition: Compensation for expected loss of purchasing power due to inflation over the investment horizon.

Measurement: Reflects the average inflation rate expected over the maturity of the debt instrument.

Key Consideration: Inflation reduces the purchasing power of a currency unit - the amount of goods and services that can be purchased. Investors require compensation to maintain real purchasing power.

Example 3: Inflation Impact

Scenario:

  • Investment amount: EUR 1,000
  • Investment period: 1 year
  • Expected inflation: 3%

Analysis:

To maintain purchasing power, the investment must return at least EUR 1,030. If the investor receives only EUR 1,000 after one year, the real return is negative - the money buys 3% less in goods and services.

Required nominal return: At minimum, must exceed 3% to compensate for inflation.

3.3 Default Risk Premium

Definition: Compensation for the possibility that the borrower will fail to make promised payments at the contracted time and in the contracted amount.

Measurement: Reflected in credit spreads - the difference in yield between a risky security and a comparable risk-free security.

Factors Affecting Default Risk Premium:

  • Borrower's financial strength (leverage, profitability, cash flow)
  • Industry and business risk
  • Economic cycle position
  • Credit rating
  • Collateral and covenants

Credit Rating Impact

Credit rating agencies (e.g., Moody's, S&P, Fitch) assess default risk. Ratings range from highest quality (AAA/Aaa) to default (D). Lower ratings require higher default risk premiums.

3.4 Liquidity Premium

Definition: Compensation for the risk of loss relative to an investment's fair value if the investment needs to be converted to cash quickly.

Drivers: Transaction costs, market depth, bid-ask spreads, and price impact of trading.

Examples of Liquidity Differences:

  • Highly liquid: US Treasury bills, large-cap stocks on major exchanges
  • Less liquid: Small-cap stocks, corporate bonds of smaller issuers
  • Illiquid: Real estate, private equity, thinly traded securities

Example 4: Liquidity Premium

Compare two bonds with identical characteristics except liquidity:

Characteristic US Treasury Bond Small Company Bond
Maturity 5 years 5 years
Default Risk None None (government guaranteed)
Daily Trading Volume $500 million $100,000
Yield 3.5% 4.2%

Liquidity Premium: 4.2% - 3.5% = 0.7% (70 basis points)

The small company bond must offer a higher yield to compensate for the difficulty and cost of selling the position if needed.

3.5 Maturity Premium

Definition: Compensation for the increased sensitivity of a security's market value to changes in market interest rates as maturity extends.

Relationship: Generally, longer-maturity securities have higher interest rate risk and thus require higher returns.

Underlying Factors:

  • Interest rate risk increases with maturity
  • Uncertainty about future economic conditions
  • Reinvestment risk considerations
  • Investor preferences for shorter investment horizons

4. Nominal Risk-Free Interest Rate

The nominal risk-free interest rate is a crucial benchmark in finance, representing the return on a risk-free investment before accounting for additional risk factors.

Precise Formula:

(1 + Nominal risk-free rate) = (1 + Real risk-free rate)(1 + Inflation premium)

Linear Approximation:

Nominal risk-free rate ≈ Real risk-free rate + Inflation premium

Note: The approximation is commonly used in practice and is reasonably accurate for low to moderate inflation rates.

Example 5: Nominal vs. Real Risk-Free Rate

Given:

  • Real risk-free rate = 2.0%
  • Expected inflation = 3.5%

Precise Calculation:

(1 + Nominal) = (1.020)(1.035) = 1.0557

Nominal risk-free rate = 5.57%

Approximation:

Nominal ≈ 2.0% + 3.5% = 5.5%

Difference: 0.07% (7 basis points) - negligible for most applications

4.1 Proxies for Nominal Risk-Free Rate

In practice, short-term government securities serve as proxies for the nominal risk-free rate:

Country Security Typical Maturities
United States Treasury Bills (T-bills) 4, 13, 26, 52 weeks
France BTFs (Bons du Trésor) Up to 1 year
Japan Treasury Bills 6, 12 months
India Treasury Bills 91, 182, 364 days

Annualization Convention

Interest rates are typically quoted on an annual basis, even for securities with shorter maturities. For example, a 90-day T-bill quoted at 3% represents an annualized rate, not the actual 90-day return.

Actual 90-day return: Approximately 3% × (90/360) = 0.75%

5. Comprehensive Example: Decomposing Interest Rates

Example 6: Multi-Security Analysis

Consider five debt securities with different characteristics:

Investment Maturity (years) Liquidity Default Risk Interest Rate (%)
1 2 High Low 2.0
2 2 Low Low 2.5
3 7 Low Low r₃
4 8 High Low 4.0
5 8 Low High 6.5

Assumption: Premiums for inflation, liquidity, and default risk are constant across all time horizons.

Problem 1: Identify the Liquidity Premium

Analysis: Compare Investments 1 and 2, which differ only in liquidity:

  • Investment 1 (High liquidity): 2.0%
  • Investment 2 (Low liquidity): 2.5%
  • Liquidity Premium: 2.5% - 2.0% = 0.5%

Problem 2: Determine the Default Risk Premium

Analysis: Compare Investments 4 and 5:

  • Both have 8-year maturity
  • Investment 4: High liquidity, Low default risk = 4.0%
  • Investment 5: Low liquidity, High default risk = 6.5%
  • Total difference: 6.5% - 4.0% = 2.5%
  • Liquidity premium component: 0.5% (from Problem 1)
  • Default risk premium: 2.5% - 0.5% = 2.0%

Problem 3: Estimate r₃

Lower Bound Analysis:

Investment 3 has longer maturity than Investment 2, so must offer higher return:

r₃ > 2.5%

Upper Bound Analysis:

If Investment 3 had high liquidity, it would be comparable to Investment 4 except for shorter maturity, suggesting r₃ < 4.0%.

Since Investment 3 actually has low liquidity, we must add the liquidity premium:

Expected r₃ < 4.0% + 0.5% = 4.5%

Conclusion:

2.5% < r₃ < 4.5%

Most likely range: 3.0% - 4.0%, reflecting moderate maturity premium relative to 2-year securities and the liquidity premium.

6. Dynamic Nature of Interest Rates

Key Insight: Continuous Fluctuation

All components of interest rates - the real risk-free rate and all risk premiums - vary continuously over time in response to:

  • Changes in economic fundamentals
  • Shifts in market expectations
  • Monetary policy adjustments
  • Credit market conditions
  • Liquidity conditions in financial markets

Consequently, observed interest rates fluctuate, with the magnitude of changes dependent on various economic factors and their expected future evolution.

7. Summary and Exam Considerations

Essential Concepts

  1. Time Value of Money: Foundation principle that cash today is preferred to cash in the future
  2. Three Interpretations: Interest rates serve as required returns, discount rates, and opportunity costs
  3. Five Components: Real risk-free rate + Inflation + Default + Liquidity + Maturity premiums
  4. Nominal Risk-Free Rate: Combination of real risk-free rate and inflation premium; serves as benchmark
  5. Government Securities: Short-term government debt represents proxy for nominal risk-free rate
  6. Dynamic Nature: All rate components fluctuate based on economic conditions and expectations

Exam Strategy

Analytical Framework: When evaluating interest rates on exam questions:

  1. Identify which securities are being compared
  2. Note which characteristics differ between securities
  3. Isolate the premium associated with each differentiating factor
  4. Apply logical relationships (e.g., higher risk → higher premium)
  5. Check reasonableness of results
