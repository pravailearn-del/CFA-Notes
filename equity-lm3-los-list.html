Market Efficiency

Module Overview: This module explores the efficient market hypothesis and its implications for investment analysis and portfolio management. Learn about different forms of market efficiency, market anomalies, and behavioral finance concepts.

by Sean Cleary, PhD, CFA, Howard J. Atkinson, CIMA, ICD.D, CFA, and Pamela Peterson Drake, PhD, CFA. Sean Cleary, PhD, CFA, is at Queen's University (Canada). Howard J. Atkinson, CIMA, ICD.D, CFA, is at Horizons ETF Management (Canada) Inc. (Canada). Pamela Peterson Drake, PhD, CFA, is at James Madison University (USA).

Learning Outcome Statements (LOS)

LOS 3.a Coming Soon
Describe market efficiency and related concepts, including their importance to investment practitioners
Understand that market efficiency refers to the extent to which security prices reflect all available information. Learn why efficiency matters for portfolio management, security analysis, and active vs. passive investment strategies. Explore the relationship between information dissemination speed, transaction costs, and market efficiency. Understand implications for the possibility of earning abnormal returns.

LOS 3.b Coming Soon
Contrast market value and intrinsic value
Learn the distinction between market value (current trading price determined by supply and demand) and intrinsic value (fundamental value based on analysis of cash flows, risk, and growth prospects). Understand that in efficient markets, market value should equal intrinsic value. Analyze how inefficiencies create opportunities when market and intrinsic values diverge.

LOS 3.c Coming Soon
Explain factors that affect a market's efficiency
Identify factors that enhance market efficiency: number of market participants, availability of information, low impediments to trading (transaction costs, taxes), and low limits to arbitrage. Understand factors that reduce efficiency: information asymmetries, behavioral biases, market frictions, and restrictions on short selling. Analyze how market structure and regulation affect efficiency levels.

LOS 3.d Coming Soon
Contrast weak-form, semi-strong-form, and strong-form market efficiency
Master the three forms of the Efficient Market Hypothesis (EMH): Weak-form efficiency (prices reflect all past market data, technical analysis cannot consistently generate excess returns), semi-strong-form efficiency (prices reflect all publicly available information, fundamental analysis cannot consistently beat the market), and strong-form efficiency (prices reflect all information including private/insider information, even insiders cannot consistently profit). Understand the cumulative nature of these forms.

LOS 3.e Coming Soon
Explain the implications of each form of market efficiency for fundamental analysis, technical analysis, and the choice between active and passive portfolio management
Understand that if markets are weak-form efficient, technical analysis provides no advantage; if semi-strong-form efficient, fundamental analysis provides no consistent advantage; if strong-form efficient, even insider information provides no advantage. Learn implications for active management (attempting to beat the market through security selection or market timing) versus passive management (tracking a market index). Analyze cost-benefit considerations for active strategies in different efficiency scenarios.

LOS 3.f Coming Soon
Describe market anomalies
Learn about persistent patterns that appear to contradict market efficiency: calendar anomalies (January effect, day-of-the-week effect), momentum and reversal effects, value vs. growth anomalies, small-cap effect, and post-earnings announcement drift. Understand debates about whether these anomalies represent true inefficiencies, risk premiums, or data mining artifacts. Analyze whether anomalies persist after being documented or disappear due to arbitrage.

LOS 3.g Coming Soon
Describe behavioral finance and its potential relevance to understanding market anomalies
Explore behavioral finance concepts that explain why markets may not be fully efficient: loss aversion (asymmetric response to gains vs. losses), overconfidence (excessive faith in one's abilities), representativeness (drawing conclusions from small samples), mental accounting (treating money differently based on source), herding behavior (following the crowd), and anchoring (over-reliance on initial information). Understand how these biases can lead to persistent pricing anomalies and market inefficiencies that create opportunities for disciplined investors.
