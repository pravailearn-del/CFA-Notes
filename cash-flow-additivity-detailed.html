Cash Flow Additivity Principle

Learning Outcome Statement (LOS)

Explain the cash flow additivity principle, its importance for the no-arbitrage condition, and its use in calculating implied forward interest rates, forward exchange rates, and option values.

I. Fundamental Concepts

Definition of Cash Flow Additivity

The cash flow additivity principle states that the present value of any stream of future cash flows indexed at the same point in time equals the sum of the present values of the individual cash flows.

Mathematical Expression:

For a series of cash flows CF‚ÇÅ, CF‚ÇÇ, ..., CF‚Çô occurring at times t‚ÇÅ, t‚ÇÇ, ..., t‚Çô:

PV(Total) = PV(CF‚ÇÅ) + PV(CF‚ÇÇ) + ... + PV(CF‚Çô) Where: PV(CF·µ¢) = CF·µ¢ / (1 + r)^t·µ¢ And r is the appropriate discount rate for each cash flow
Key Implication: Cash flows may be segregated, combined, or rearranged without altering their aggregate present value, provided the timing and risk characteristics remain constant.

The No-Arbitrage Condition

No-arbitrage condition: A market condition where no possibility exists to earn a riskless profit in the absence of transaction costs and with zero initial investment.

Relationship to Cash Flow Additivity:

The cash flow additivity principle is fundamental to ensuring market prices satisfy the no-arbitrage condition. When cash flows can be decomposed and recombined, any violation of additivity would create arbitrage opportunities that market participants would immediately exploit, driving prices back to equilibrium.

II. Application to Implied Forward Interest Rates

Conceptual Framework

An implied forward interest rate is the future interest rate implied by current spot rates such that investors are indifferent between two alternative investment strategies:

  1. Investing for the longer maturity at the long-term rate
  2. Investing for the shorter maturity and reinvesting at the forward rate

The cash flow additivity principle ensures these strategies have equivalent present values under no-arbitrage conditions.

General Forward Rate Formula: For a forward rate F‚Çê,·µ¶ starting at time a and ending at time b: (1 + r·µ¶)^b = (1 + r‚Çê)^a √ó (1 + F‚Çê,·µ¶)^(b-a) Solving for F‚Çê,·µ¶: F‚Çê,·µ¶ = [(1 + r·µ¶)^b / (1 + r‚Çê)^a]^(1/(b-a)) - 1 Where: - r‚Çê = spot rate for maturity a - r·µ¶ = spot rate for maturity b - F‚Çê,·µ¶ = forward rate from time a to time b

Arbitrage Verification

If the actual forward rate available in the market differs from the implied forward rate, an arbitrage opportunity exists:

If actual forward rate > implied forward rate:

  • Borrow at the 2-year rate
  • Lend at the 1-year rate and lock in lending at the higher forward rate
  • Profit = (Actual F‚ÇÅ,‚ÇÅ - Implied F‚ÇÅ,‚ÇÅ) √ó Invested Amount / (1 + r‚ÇÅ)

If actual forward rate < implied forward rate:

  • Borrow at the 1-year rate, then borrow again at the actual forward rate
  • Lend at the 2-year rate
  • Profit flows in the opposite direction

III. Application to Forward Exchange Rates

Interest Rate Parity

The covered interest rate parity relationship ensures no-arbitrage between currency markets and interest rate markets. It states that the forward exchange rate must adjust to offset interest rate differentials between two countries.

Forward Exchange Rate Formula: F = S √ó [(1 + r_domestic) / (1 + r_foreign)] Where: F = Forward exchange rate (domestic currency per foreign currency) S = Spot exchange rate (domestic currency per foreign currency) r_domestic = Domestic risk-free interest rate r_foreign = Foreign risk-free interest rate Alternative continuous compounding form: F = S √ó e^((r_domestic - r_foreign) √ó T)

Example 3: Forward Exchange Rate Calculation

Given Market Data (as of mid-June 2022):

Parameter Value
Spot GBP/USD Rate 1.2602 (USD per GBP)
UK Risk-Free Rate (rGBP) 1.55% (continuously compounded)
US Risk-Free Rate (rUSD) 2.667% (continuously compounded)
Time Horizon 1 year

Required: Calculate the no-arbitrage one-year forward USD/GBP exchange rate.

Solution:

Step 1: Identify equivalent strategies

Domestic Strategy (Invest in UK):

  • Invest GBP 1,000 at rGBP = 1.55%
  • After 1 year: GBP 1,000 √ó e^(0.0155) = GBP 1,015.74

Foreign Strategy (Invest in US):

  • Convert GBP 1,000 to USD at spot rate: 1,000 √ó 1.2602 = USD 1,260.20
  • Invest at rUSD = 2.667%
  • After 1 year: USD 1,260.20 √ó e^(0.02667) = USD 1,294.26

Step 2: Calculate forward rate for equivalence

For no-arbitrage: GBP 1,015.74 (future value in UK) must equal USD 1,294.26 when converted Forward Rate = USD 1,294.26 / GBP 1,015.74 = 1.2742 USD/GBP

Alternative Calculation Using Formula:

F = S √ó e^((r_USD - r_GBP) √ó T) F = 1.2602 √ó e^((0.02667 - 0.0155) √ó 1) F = 1.2602 √ó e^(0.01117) F = 1.2602 √ó 1.01123 F = 1.2742 USD/GBP

Interpretation:

  • The USD depreciates on a forward basis (forward rate 1.2742 > spot rate 1.2602)
  • This compensates for the higher US interest rate (2.667% vs 1.55%)
  • The interest rate differential of 1.117% translates to approximately 1.12% forward premium for GBP
  • This relationship prevents covered interest arbitrage

Example 4: Detecting Arbitrage Opportunities

Scenario: Market quotes vs. theoretical prices

Parameter Value
Spot EUR/USD 1.0800
Market Forward Rate (1-year) 1.0950
EUR Interest Rate 3.0%
USD Interest Rate 4.5%

Analysis:

Step 1: Calculate theoretical forward rate

F_theoretical = S √ó [(1 + r_USD) / (1 + r_EUR)] F_theoretical = 1.0800 √ó [(1.045) / (1.030)] F_theoretical = 1.0800 √ó 1.01456 F_theoretical = 1.0957 USD/EUR

Step 2: Compare to market forward rate

  • Market forward: 1.0950
  • Theoretical forward: 1.0957
  • Difference: 0.0007 (EUR is underpriced in forward market)

Step 3: Arbitrage strategy

Since market forward rate < theoretical forward rate, EUR is cheap on forward basis:

  1. Today (t=0):
    • Borrow USD 1,000,000 at 4.5%
    • Convert to EUR: 1,000,000/1.0800 = EUR 925,926
    • Invest EUR at 3.0%
    • Enter forward contract to sell EUR at 1.0950
  2. One year later (t=1):
    • EUR investment matures: 925,926 √ó 1.03 = EUR 953,704
    • Convert to USD at forward: 953,704 √ó 1.0950 = USD 1,044,306
    • Repay USD loan: 1,000,000 √ó 1.045 = USD 1,045,000
    • Net profit: USD 1,044,306 - USD 1,045,000 = -USD 694 (loss)

Note: The negative result indicates we chose the wrong direction. Reverse the strategy:

  1. Borrow EUR at 3.0%, convert to USD, invest at 4.5%
  2. Enter forward to buy EUR at 1.0950
  3. This captures the mispricing and generates profit

Key Insights on Forward Exchange Rates

  • Currency with higher interest rate trades at a forward discount: Its forward rate < spot rate
  • Currency with lower interest rate trades at a forward premium: Its forward rate > spot rate
  • The relationship is precise: Small deviations create arbitrage opportunities
  • Transaction costs and capital controls may create bounds within which no-arbitrage holds
  • For exam purposes: Covered interest parity assumes perfect capital mobility and no transaction costs

IV. Application to Option Pricing

Replicating Portfolio Approach

The cash flow additivity principle enables option pricing through construction of a replicating portfolio - a combination of the underlying asset and risk-free borrowing/lending that perfectly mimics the option's payoff structure.

Core Principle: If two portfolios generate identical cash flows in all possible future states, they must have the same present value to prevent arbitrage.

One-Period Binomial Model

The binomial model assumes the underlying asset can take only two possible values in the next period:

  • Su: Asset price if it moves up
  • Sd: Asset price if it moves down

From current price S‚ÇÄ, the option values are:

  • cu: Call option value if asset moves up
  • cd: Call option value if asset moves down
Call Option Replication Formula: Create portfolio: Long Œî shares + Short (borrow) Amount B Portfolio value at t=0: V‚ÇÄ = Œî¬∑S‚ÇÄ - B Portfolio values at t=1: - Up state: V_u = Œî¬∑S_u - B(1+r) - Down state: V_d = Œî¬∑S_d - B(1+r) For replication, set V_u = c_u and V_d = c_d Solving simultaneously: Œî = (c_u - c_d) / (S_u - S_d) [Hedge ratio] B = [S_u¬∑c_d - S_d¬∑c_u] / [(S_u - S_d)(1+r)] Call option value: c‚ÇÄ = Œî¬∑S‚ÇÄ - B

Example 5: Pricing a Call Option (Detailed)

Given:

Parameter Value
Current stock price (S‚ÇÄ) CNY 40
Up movement +40% ‚Üí Su = CNY 56
Down movement -20% ‚Üí Sd = CNY 32
Strike price (X) CNY 50
Risk-free rate (r) 5%
Time to expiration 1 period

Required: Calculate the fair value of the call option.

Solution:

Step 1: Determine option payoffs at maturity

Up state (S_u = CNY 56): c_u = Max(0, S_u - X) = Max(0, 56 - 50) = CNY 6 Down state (S_d = CNY 32): c_d = Max(0, S_d - X) = Max(0, 32 - 50) = CNY 0

Step 2: Calculate hedge ratio (Œî)

Œî = (c_u - c_d) / (S_u - S_d) Œî = (6 - 0) / (56 - 32) Œî = 6 / 24 = 0.25

Interpretation: Hold 0.25 shares to replicate the option.

Step 3: Verify replication portfolio payoffs

Up state value: 0.25 √ó 56 = CNY 14.00 Down state value: 0.25 √ó 32 = CNY 8.00 These values must be reduced by borrowed amount with interest to match option payoffs (6 and 0) Therefore, borrowed amount B satisfies: 14.00 - B(1.05) = 6 B(1.05) = 8.00 B = 7.619

Step 4: Calculate current call option value

c‚ÇÄ = Œî¬∑S‚ÇÄ - B c‚ÇÄ = 0.25 √ó 40 - 7.619 c‚ÇÄ = 10.00 - 7.619 c‚ÇÄ = CNY 2.38

Verification:

Alternative: Discount the risk-free payoff Portfolio value in 1 period = CNY 8 (in both states) Present value = 8 / 1.05 = CNY 7.619 Plus stock position: 0.25 √ó 40 = CNY 10 Net investment = 10 - 7.619 = CNY 2.38 ‚úì

Key Insights:

  • The option price does not depend on probabilities of up/down moves
  • Only requires: current stock price, possible future prices, strike, and risk-free rate
  • The replicating portfolio creates a synthetic option
  • Any price other than CNY 2.38 creates an arbitrage opportunity

Example 6: Pricing a Put Option

Given: Same underlying asset parameters as Example 5

  • S‚ÇÄ = CNY 40
  • Su = CNY 56, Sd = CNY 32
  • Strike price X = CNY 50
  • r = 5%

Required: Calculate the fair value of the put option.

Solution:

Step 1: Determine put option payoffs

Up state (S_u = CNY 56): p_u = Max(0, X - S_u) = Max(0, 50 - 56) = CNY 0 Down state (S_d = CNY 32): p_d = Max(0, X - S_d) = Max(0, 50 - 32) = CNY 18

Step 2: Calculate hedge ratio

For puts, we buy stock and sell (write) the put: Œî = (p_u - p_d) / (S_u - S_d) Œî = (0 - 18) / (56 - 32) Œî = -18 / 24 = -0.75

Interpretation: The negative sign indicates we need to short 0.75 shares. However, for a risk-free portfolio that buys the put, we buy 0.75 shares.

Step 3: Construct risk-free portfolio

Portfolio: Long 0.75 shares + Long 1 put option

Up state: 0.75 √ó 56 + 0 = CNY 42 Down state: 0.75 √ó 32 + 18 = CNY 42 Risk-free payoff = CNY 42 in both states

Step 4: Calculate put option value

Present value of portfolio = 42 / 1.05 = CNY 40 Portfolio cost: 0.75 √ó S‚ÇÄ + p‚ÇÄ = 0.75 √ó 40 + p‚ÇÄ = 30 + p‚ÇÄ Setting equal: 30 + p‚ÇÄ = 40 p‚ÇÄ = CNY 10.00

Verification using Put-Call Parity:

Put-Call Parity: p‚ÇÄ + S‚ÇÄ = c‚ÇÄ + X/(1+r) From Example 5: c‚ÇÄ = CNY 2.38 p‚ÇÄ = c‚ÇÄ + X/(1+r) - S‚ÇÄ p‚ÇÄ = 2.38 + 50/1.05 - 40 p‚ÇÄ = 2.38 + 47.62 - 40 p‚ÇÄ = CNY 10.00 ‚úì

Critical Concepts in Option Pricing via Cash Flow Additivity

  1. Risk-neutral valuation: The replication approach does not require knowing actual probabilities of price movements
  2. No-arbitrage pricing: Option value = Cost of replicating portfolio, by the no-arbitrage principle
  3. Hedge ratio: Represents the number of shares needed per option to create a risk-free position
  4. Dynamic hedging: In practice, the hedge ratio changes as the stock price and time change, requiring continuous rebalancing
  5. Extensions: Multi-period binomial trees build on this framework by working backwards from expiration
  6. Black-Scholes model: Represents the continuous-time limit of the binomial model

Example 7: Multi-Asset Arbitrage Analysis

Scenario: A financial analyst is evaluating opportunities across bond, currency, and equity derivative markets.

Given Market Data:

Interest Rate Markets

1-year EUR LIBOR 2.10%

1-year Forward EUR/USD 1.0965
Equity Market

1-year call option (Strike $105) $8.50
Expected price in 1 year (up) $125
Expected price in 1 year (down) $85

Required:

  1. Calculate the implied 1-year forward rate starting in 1 year for USD
  2. Determine if the EUR/USD forward rate satisfies covered interest parity
  3. Calculate the fair value of the call option and identify any mispricing

Solution:

Part 1: Implied Forward Interest Rate

F‚ÇÅ,‚ÇÅ = [(1 + r‚ÇÇ)¬≤ / (1 + r‚ÇÅ)] - 1 F‚ÇÅ,‚ÇÅ = [(1.0380)¬≤ / (1.0320)] - 1 F‚ÇÅ,‚ÇÅ = [1.077444 / 1.0320] - 1 F‚ÇÅ,‚ÇÅ = 1.04403 - 1 = 0.04403 = 4.40%

Interpretation: The market is pricing in a 1-year rate of 4.40% beginning one year from today, representing a significant expected increase from current 1-year rate of 3.20%.

Part 2: Covered Interest Parity Analysis

Theoretical Forward Rate: F_theoretical = S √ó [(1 + r_USD) / (1 + r_EUR)] F_theoretical = 1.0850 √ó [(1.0320) / (1.0210)] F_theoretical = 1.0850 √ó 1.01077 F_theoretical = 1.0967 USD/EUR

Comparison:

  • Market forward rate: 1.0965
  • Theoretical forward rate: 1.0967
  • Difference: 0.0002 (20 pips)

Conclusion: The forward rate is slightly undervalued but the difference (20 pips) is likely within the bid-ask spread and transaction costs. No practical arbitrage opportunity exists.

Part 3: Call Option Valuation

Step 1: Determine option payoffs

Up state (S_u = $125): c_u = Max(0, 125 - 105) = $20 Down state (S_d = $85): c_d = Max(0, 85 - 105) = $0

Step 2: Calculate hedge ratio

Œî = (c_u - c_d) / (S_u - S_d) Œî = (20 - 0) / (125 - 85) Œî = 20 / 40 = 0.50

Step 3: Determine borrowing amount

Portfolio values must match option payoffs:

Up state: 0.50 √ó 125 - B(1.032) = 20 62.50 - B(1.032) = 20 B(1.032) = 42.50 B = 41.18 Down state: 0.50 √ó 85 - B(1.032) = 0 42.50 - B(1.032) = 0 B(1.032) = 42.50 B = 41.18 ‚úì (consistent)

Step 4: Calculate fair option value

c‚ÇÄ = Œî¬∑S‚ÇÄ - B c‚ÇÄ = 0.50 √ó 100 - 41.18 c‚ÇÄ = 50.00 - 41.18 c‚ÇÄ = $8.82

Mispricing Analysis:

  • Market price: $8.50
  • Fair value: $8.82
  • Difference: $0.32 undervalued

Arbitrage Strategy:

  1. Buy: The undervalued call option at $8.50
  2. Short: 0.50 shares of XYZ stock (receive $50.00)
  3. Invest: $41.50 at 3.20% risk-free rate
  4. Net today: -8.50 + 50.00 - 41.50 = $0 (self-financing)

After one year:

  • Investment grows to: $41.50 √ó 1.032 = $42.83
  • If stock rises to $125: Pay $62.50 for short stock, receive $20 from option, plus $42.83 = $0.33 profit
  • If stock falls to $85: Pay $42.50 for short stock, receive $0 from option, plus $42.83 = $0.33 profit

Conclusion: The option is undervalued by $0.32, creating a riskless arbitrage profit.
