Capital Investments and Capital Allocation

Module Overview: This module covers the process of evaluating and selecting long-term investment projects. Understanding capital budgeting techniques including NPV, IRR, and ROIC, as well as real options and common pitfalls, is essential for assessing management's ability to create value through capital allocation decisions.

Learning Outcome Statements (LOS)

LOS 5.a Coming Soon
Describe types of capital investments
Identify major categories of capital investments: replacement projects (maintaining operations or cost reduction through new equipment), expansion projects (increasing capacity, entering new markets, new product lines), regulatory/compliance projects (meeting safety, environmental, or legal requirements), and other projects (research and development, IT systems, acquisitions). Understand that different project types have varying risk profiles, evaluation criteria, and strategic importance. Recognize that some investments are mandatory while others are discretionary and compete for limited capital resources.

LOS 5.b Coming Soon
Describe the capital allocation process, calculate net present value (NPV), internal rate of return (IRR), and return on invested capital (ROIC), and contrast their use in capital allocation
Master the capital budgeting process: idea generation, project analysis, capital allocation planning, and post-audit review. Calculate NPV = Sum of [CF_t / (1+r)^t] - Initial Investment; accept if NPV > 0. Calculate IRR where NPV = 0; accept if IRR > required return. Calculate ROIC = NOPAT / Invested Capital to assess returns on deployed capital. Compare methods: NPV is theoretically superior (accounts for scale, assumes reinvestment at WACC), IRR is intuitive but has multiple IRR and scale problems, ROIC evaluates historical performance. Understand conflicts between NPV and IRR rankings for mutually exclusive projects.

LOS 5.c Coming Soon
Describe principles of capital allocation and common capital allocation pitfalls
Understand key principles: decisions based on incremental cash flows (ignore sunk costs, include opportunity costs and externalities), cash flows evaluated on after-tax basis, proper timing of cash flows, consistent treatment of inflation, and appropriate risk-adjusted discount rates. Identify common pitfalls: pet projects of senior management, basing decisions on EPS or accounting income rather than cash flows, using IRR when NPV conflicts, not considering all alternatives, excessive optimism in forecasts, failing to account for real options value, inadequate post-audit reviews, and capital rationing constraints limiting value maximization.

LOS 5.d Coming Soon
Describe types of real options relevant to capital investments
Understand real options as managerial flexibility embedded in capital projects. Identify types: timing options (delay investment until better information), abandonment options (terminate project if conditions worsen, limiting downside), expansion options (scale up if conditions favorable, capturing upside), flexibility options (switch inputs/outputs or production methods), and fundamental options (strategic investments enabling future opportunities). Recognize that traditional NPV analysis may undervalue projects with significant option value. Understand that real options are most valuable when uncertainty is high, time to expiration is long, and management has flexibility to respond to new information.
