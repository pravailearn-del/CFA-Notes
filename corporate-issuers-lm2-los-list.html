Investors and Other Stakeholders

Module Overview: This module explores the diverse groups that have interests in corporate activities, including investors, creditors, employees, customers, and communities. Understanding stakeholder relationships and their sometimes competing interests is crucial for analyzing corporate decision-making and governance effectiveness.

Learning Outcome Statements (LOS)

LOS 2.a Coming Soon
Compare the financial claims and motivations of lenders and shareholders
Analyze the distinct positions of lenders (creditors) versus shareholders (equity holders). Lenders have contractual claims with fixed payments, priority in bankruptcy, limited upside potential, and focus on downside protection and covenant compliance. Shareholders have residual claims, unlimited upside potential, voting rights, but stand last in liquidation. Compare risk-return profiles, time horizons, and how these differences create potential conflicts. Understand how capital structure affects the balance of power between debt and equity holders.

LOS 2.b Coming Soon
Describe a company's stakeholder groups and compare their interests
Identify key stakeholder groups: shareholders (maximizing share value, dividends), creditors (loan repayment, creditworthiness), employees (job security, compensation, working conditions), management (compensation, job security, reputation), customers (quality products, fair pricing), suppliers (payment terms, long-term relationships), government (tax revenue, compliance, employment), and communities (economic development, environmental protection). Understand that stakeholder interests often conflict, requiring companies to balance competing demands. Analyze how stakeholder management affects long-term firm value.

LOS 2.c Coming Soon
Describe environmental, social, and governance factors of corporate issuers considered by investors
Understand ESG integration in investment analysis. Environmental factors include carbon emissions, resource depletion, waste management, and climate change impact. Social factors cover labor practices, human rights, product safety, diversity and inclusion, and community relations. Governance factors encompass board structure, executive compensation, shareholder rights, audit quality, and business ethics. Analyze how ESG factors can affect long-term financial performance, risk management, regulatory compliance, and reputation. Understand different ESG investment approaches including screening, integration, and impact investing.
