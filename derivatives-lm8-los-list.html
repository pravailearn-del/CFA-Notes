Explain the exercise value, moneyness, and time value of an option
Master option value components. Exercise value (intrinsic value): the value if exercised immediately‚Äîfor calls: max(0, S - X); for puts: max(0, X - S), where S is spot price and X is strike price. Moneyness: relationship between spot and strike‚Äîin-the-money (ITM): exercise value > 0; at-the-money (ATM): S ‚âà X; out-of-the-money (OTM): exercise value = 0. Time value: option price minus intrinsic value, representing the value of optionality‚Äîthe possibility that the option will move further ITM before expiration. Time value is highest for ATM options and decreases as options move deeper ITM or OTM, approaching zero at expiration.

LOS 8.b Coming Soon
Contrast the use of arbitrage and replication concepts in pricing forward commitments and contingent claims
Compare pricing approaches for forwards versus options. For forward commitments: arbitrage and replication directly determine a single price‚Äîforwards can be perfectly replicated by buying the underlying and borrowing, creating exact arbitrage bounds. For contingent claims (options): arbitrage establishes price boundaries (upper and lower bounds) but doesn't determine exact price because options cannot be perfectly replicated with static positions due to their non-linear payoffs. Options require dynamic replication (continuously adjusting hedge ratios) or probability-based models (like Black-Scholes or binomial models) for precise pricing. Both use no-arbitrage principles, but options need more sophisticated techniques.
