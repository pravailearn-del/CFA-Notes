üìã Introduction

Financial assets are frequently defined in terms of their return and risk characteristics. Comparison along these two dimensions simplifies the process of building a portfolio from among all available assets. This lesson focuses on computing, evaluating, and comparing various measures of return.

üí∞ Sources of Returns from Financial Assets

Financial assets normally generate two types of return for investors:

  1. Periodic Income: Cash dividends from stocks or interest payments from bonds
  2. Capital Gains/Losses: Changes in the price of the financial asset

Important Considerations:

  • Some financial assets provide return through only one mechanism (e.g., non-dividend-paying stocks rely solely on price appreciation)
  • Other assets only generate periodic income (e.g., defined benefit pension plans and retirement annuities)
  • Most assets provide a combination of both income and capital appreciation

üìä 1. Holding Period Return (HPR)

Definition

A holding period return (R) is the return earned from holding an asset for a single specified period of time. The period may be one day, one week, one month, five years, or any specified period.

Example 1: Basic HPR Calculation

Scenario: Asset purchased at time t = 0 for a price of 100 and sold at time t = 1 for 105, with income of 2 units at time t = 1.

Capital Gain Component:

(105 - 100) / 100 = 5%

Income Yield Component:

2 / 100 = 2%

Total Holding Period Return:

[(105 - 100) + 2] / 100 = 7%
Key Observations:
  • For ease of illustration, we often assume income is paid at the end of the period (t = 1)
  • If income was received before t = 1, the holding period return could be higher if we had reinvested the income for the remainder of the period
  • Return can be expressed as decimals (0.07), fractions (7/100), or percentages (7%) - they are all equivalent

Example 6: Comprehensive Comparison

Key Observations:

  • The actual balance at the end of Year 3 is EUR 0.8573 (a loss)
  • Using the 4% arithmetic mean suggests a balance of EUR 1.1249 (a significant overestimate)
  • Using the -5% geometric mean results in EUR 0.8574 (nearly identical to actual)

Critical Insight: The arithmetic return is biased upward unless each of the underlying holding period returns are equal. The bias in arithmetic mean returns is particularly severe if holding period returns are a mix of both positive and negative returns.

Mathematical Relationship

Unless all observations are equal:

Geometric Mean < Arithmetic Mean

The gap between the two increases with the volatility of returns. The more variable the returns (especially with negative returns), the larger the difference.

üéì Key Takeaways

Essential Points to Remember

  1. Holding Period Return: Measures return over any single specified period; can be compounded for multi-period returns
  2. Arithmetic Mean: Simple average; useful for forecasting but overstates actual growth when returns vary
  3. Geometric Mean: Compound average; accurately reflects actual investment growth over time
  4. Compounding Effect: Multi-year returns must be compounded, not simply added together
  5. Volatility Penalty: Higher volatility creates a larger gap between arithmetic and geometric means
  6. Always True: Geometric Mean ‚â§ Arithmetic Mean (equal only when all returns are identical)
  7. Practical Application: Use geometric mean for historical performance; use arithmetic mean for forward-looking estimates
For the Exam:
  • Pay attention to whether questions ask for "average return" (arithmetic) or "compound annual growth rate" (geometric)
  • When calculating multi-period returns, remember to compound using (1 + R) format
  • Be prepared to explain why geometric mean better represents actual portfolio growth
  • Understand that arithmetic mean has an upward bias, especially with volatile returns
