Equity Valuation: Concepts and Basic Tools

Module Overview: This module covers fundamental valuation concepts and techniques including dividend discount models, free cash flow models, and relative valuation using multiples. Essential foundation for equity analysis and investment decisions.

by John J. Nagorniak, CFA, and Stephen E. Wilcox, PhD, CFA. John J. Nagorniak, CFA (USA). Stephen E. Wilcox, PhD, CFA, is at Minnesota State University, Mankato (USA).

Learning Outcome Statements (LOS)

LOS 8.a Coming Soon
Evaluate whether a security, given its current market price and a value estimate, is overvalued, fairly valued, or undervalued by the market
Learn to compare intrinsic value (estimated fundamental value based on analysis) with market price to make investment decisions. If intrinsic value > market price, the security is undervalued (buy signal). If intrinsic value < market price, overvalued (sell signal). If intrinsic value ‚âà market price, fairly valued (hold). Understand that valuation requires judgment about assumptions and that different analysts may reach different conclusions. Recognize importance of margin of safety to account for estimation uncertainty.

LOS 8.b Coming Soon
Describe major categories of equity valuation models
Understand the three major valuation approaches: (1) Present value models - discount expected cash flows (dividend discount models, free cash flow models), (2) Multiplier models - relative valuation using ratios (P/E, P/B, EV/EBITDA), and (3) Asset-based models - net asset value approaches. Learn when each category is most appropriate: present value for going concerns with predictable cash flows, multiplier for quick comparisons and when comparable companies exist, asset-based for companies with significant tangible assets or in liquidation.

LOS 8.c Coming Soon
Describe regular cash dividends, extra dividends, stock dividends, stock splits, reverse stock splits, and share repurchases
Learn corporate actions affecting shareholders: Regular dividends (periodic cash payments), extra dividends (one-time special payments), stock dividends (additional shares paid as dividends, no cash), stock splits (increase shares outstanding, reduce price per share proportionally), reverse stock splits (decrease shares, increase price), and share repurchases (company buys back its own shares, reducing shares outstanding). Understand that stock dividends and splits don't change total value but affect per-share metrics. Repurchases represent alternative to dividends for returning cash to shareholders.

LOS 8.d Coming Soon
Describe dividend payment chronology
Understand the sequence of dividend-related dates: Declaration date (board announces dividend), Ex-dividend date (first day stock trades without dividend right; price typically drops by dividend amount), Record date (shareholders of record on this date receive dividend), and Payment date (dividend actually paid). Learn that to receive a dividend, investor must purchase stock before ex-dividend date. Understand that stock price adjustment on ex-dividend date reflects the value transferred to shareholders through the dividend payment.

LOS 8.e Coming Soon
Explain the rationale for using present value models to value equity and describe the dividend discount and free-cash-flow-to-equity models
Understand that present value models are based on the principle that an asset's value equals the present value of expected future cash flows. For equity, relevant cash flows are dividends (actual cash received by shareholders) or free cash flow to equity (FCFE = cash available to equity holders after all obligations met). Dividend Discount Model (DDM): V = Œ£ [D_t / (1+r)^t] where D_t is dividend in period t, r is required return. FCFE Model: V = Œ£ [FCFE_t / (1+r)^t]. These models link value directly to fundamental drivers: cash generation and risk (discount rate).

LOS 8.f Coming Soon
Explain advantages and disadvantages of each category of valuation model
Compare model strengths and weaknesses: Present value models (advantages: theoretically sound, cash-flow focused; disadvantages: sensitive to assumptions, requires forecasts, difficult for non-dividend-paying stocks), Multiplier models (advantages: simple, intuitive, useful for comparison; disadvantages: doesn't capture company-specific factors, requires comparable companies, backward-looking), Asset-based models (advantages: useful for asset-heavy companies, provides floor value; disadvantages: ignores going-concern value, book values may not reflect market values, intangibles not captured). Choose model based on company characteristics, data availability, and purpose of valuation.

LOS 8.g Coming Soon
Calculate the intrinsic value of a non-callable, non-convertible preferred stock
Learn that preferred stock with fixed perpetual dividends is valued like a perpetuity: V = D / r, where D is the annual dividend and r is the required rate of return. For example, if a preferred stock pays $5 annual dividend and the required return is 8%, value = $5 / 0.08 = $62.50. Understand that preferred stock valuation is straightforward because dividends are typically fixed and perpetual (no growth). The main challenge is determining the appropriate required return based on the preferred stock's risk characteristics.

LOS 8.h Coming Soon
Calculate and interpret the intrinsic value of an equity security based on the Gordon (constant) growth dividend discount model or a two-stage dividend discount model, as appropriate
Master the Gordon Growth Model for constant dividend growth: V = D‚ÇÅ / (r - g), where D‚ÇÅ is next year's dividend, r is required return, g is constant growth rate (note: r must exceed g). For two-stage model: V = Œ£[D_t / (1+r)^t] for t=1 to n (high growth period) + [D_(n+1) / (r-g_stable)] / (1+r)^n (terminal value). Use Gordon model for mature, stable companies with predictable growth. Use two-stage for companies transitioning from high growth to stable growth. Practice solving for value given inputs and interpreting results relative to market price.

LOS 8.i Coming Soon
Identify characteristics of companies for which the constant growth or a multistage dividend discount model is appropriate
Gordon Growth Model is appropriate for: mature companies with stable growth rates, consistent dividend policies, and predictable earnings (utilities, consumer staples). Multistage models are appropriate for: companies in transition (high growth to stable growth), companies with multiple growth phases, young companies expected to mature. DDMs generally inappropriate for: non-dividend-paying companies, companies with unpredictable dividends, high-growth companies where g > r (violates model assumptions), and companies in financial distress. Consider company life cycle stage, dividend history, and growth prospects when selecting valuation approach.

LOS 8.j Coming Soon
Explain the rationale for using price multiples to value equity, how the price to earnings multiple relates to fundamentals, and the use of multiples based on comparables
Understand that price multiples provide relative valuation by comparing a stock's price to an earnings, book value, sales, or cash flow metric. P/E ratio relates to fundamentals through Gordon Growth Model: P/E = (1-b)(1+g) / (r-g), where b is retention ratio. This shows P/E increases with higher growth (g), lower risk (lower r), and higher payout ratio (lower b). Using comparables: compare a company's multiple to peers to identify relatively cheap or expensive stocks. Select appropriate comparable companies (similar industry, size, growth, risk) and adjust for differences. Limitations: multiples don't capture company-specific factors, require comparable companies, and ratios can be distorted by accounting differences.
