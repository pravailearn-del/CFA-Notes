Understanding Money-Weighted and Time-Weighted Returns: Which One Tells Your Real Story? üéØ

üí° The Big Question

Imagine you're investing in a mutual fund. You start with ‚Çπ1,00,000, then add ‚Çπ10,00,000 after a year when the fund performs really well. But then the fund has a bad year. How do you calculate your actual return?

Here's the thing: when you add or withdraw money matters a lot! That's why we need two different ways to measure returns. Think of it like weighing yourself with and without accounting for the big meal you just ate - timing matters!

Why Do We Need Two Different Return Measures? ü§î

üé™ The Cricket Batting Average Analogy

Think of Money-Weighted Return like a cricket player's batting average that accounts for how many balls they faced in each innings. If they scored big runs when they faced many balls, their average is higher.

Time-Weighted Return is like looking at the average score per innings, regardless of how many balls were faced. It just looks at performance in each time period equally.

Both tell you something important, but different!

Money-Weighted Return: Your Personal Investment Story üíº

üéØ What Is It?

The money-weighted return tells you what you personally earned on your actual investment. It's like calculating your cricket batting average where big innings count more heavily.

If you invested a lot of money when the market was down and it then recovered, your money-weighted return will be higher. Lucky timing! But if you invested heavily right before a crash, your return will be lower. Bad timing!

Key insight: It's basically the Internal Rate of Return (IRR) of your investment cash flows.

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example: Rajesh's Mutual Fund Journey

Scenario: Rajesh invests in an HDFC mutual fund over three years:

  • Year 0 (Start): Invests ‚Çπ1,00,000
  • Year 1 (Start): Adds ‚Çπ9,50,000

The returns during each year were:

  • Year 1: -50% (market crash!)
  • Year 2: +35% (recovery)
  • Year 3: +27% (bull run)

His money-weighted return: 26.11% per year!

Why so high? Because he had only ‚Çπ1,00,000 invested during the -50% crash, but ‚Çπ10,00,000+ invested during the good years. Smart timing (or lucky!).

Time-Weighted Return: The Fair Comparison Tool ‚è∞

üéØ What Is It?

The time-weighted return measures how well the investment performed, independent of when you added or withdrew money. It's like asking "How did the fund manager do?" rather than "How did I do?"

This is the preferred method for evaluating fund managers because it's not affected by investor decisions about when to add or withdraw money (which managers can't control).

Key insight: It breaks your investment into periods and calculates the geometric mean of returns, treating each period equally.

üèè The Fair Scorecard Analogy

Imagine evaluating a cricket coach. You want to know how good the coach is, not whether players happened to perform better when more people were watching!

Time-weighted return is like looking at the team's average performance in each match, regardless of how important the match was. It's the fairest way to judge the coach's skill.

Money-weighted return would be like giving more importance to matches where more fans showed up - not really fair to the coach!

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example: Comparing Two Investors in Same Fund

The Situation: Both Priya and Amit invest in the same Tata mutual fund that has these annual returns:

  • Year 1: +20%
  • Year 2: -10%
  • Year 3: +15%

Priya's investments:

  • Starts with ‚Çπ1,00,000
  • Adds ‚Çπ5,00,000 at start of Year 2 (before the -10% year)

Amit's investments:

  • Starts with ‚Çπ1,00,000
  • Adds ‚Çπ5,00,000 at start of Year 3 (after the -10% year)

Results:

  • Priya's money-weighted return: Lower (she invested big before the bad year)
  • Amit's money-weighted return: Higher (he invested big after the bad year)
  • Time-weighted return for both: Same! Because it's measuring the fund's performance, not their timing

This is why fund managers prefer time-weighted returns - it's fair! They can't control when investors add or withdraw money.

When to Use Each One? ü§∑

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Takeaway

For evaluating portfolio managers: Use Time-Weighted Return

For knowing what YOU actually earned: Use Money-Weighted Return

It's like the difference between judging a cricket coach (time-weighted) vs. calculating your own batting average (money-weighted)!

üéì The Practical Reality

In the real world:

  • Mutual fund companies advertise their time-weighted returns (makes sense - they want to show their management skill)
  • Your actual account statement might show money-weighted returns (what you actually earned)
  • CFA exam will test you on both - so know when to use each! üòä
