Portfolio Management: An Overview

Module Overview: This module provides a comprehensive overview of the portfolio management process, different types of investors, and the asset management industry. You'll learn about the portfolio approach to investing, pension plan structures, and various pooled investment vehicles including mutual funds.

Learning Outcome Statements (LOS)

LOS 3.a Coming Soon
Describe the portfolio approach to investing
Understand the portfolio perspective, which focuses on analyzing investments as part of a whole portfolio rather than in isolation. Key principles include: evaluating assets based on their contribution to total portfolio risk and return (not standalone characteristics), recognizing diversification benefits from combining assets with low correlations, understanding that portfolio risk is less than the sum of individual asset risks, and making investment decisions considering how they affect the entire portfolio's risk-return profile. This approach contrasts with security selection in isolation and emphasizes the importance of asset allocation and diversification in achieving investment objectives.

Describe the steps in the portfolio management process
Learn the systematic portfolio management process. Step 1: Planning‚Äîunderstand client circumstances, objectives, and constraints; develop an Investment Policy Statement (IPS). Step 2: Execution‚Äîdetermine asset allocation, select securities, and implement the portfolio. Step 3: Feedback‚Äîmonitor and rebalance the portfolio, measure performance, and review client circumstances. This is an ongoing, iterative process. The IPS serves as the foundation, documenting investment objectives, risk tolerance, time horizon, liquidity needs, and constraints. Regular monitoring ensures the portfolio remains aligned with client goals, while rebalancing maintains target asset allocation as market values change.

LOS 3.c Coming Soon
Describe types of investors and distinctive characteristics and needs of each
Distinguish between investor types and their unique needs. Individual investors: personal wealth accumulation, retirement planning, tax concerns, varying risk tolerance, liquidity needs for spending. Institutional investors include: defined benefit pension plans (liability-driven, long horizon, regular outflows), defined contribution plans (participant-directed, accumulation focus), endowments and foundations (perpetual horizon, spending policies, preserve purchasing power), insurance companies (liability-matching, regulatory constraints), banks (liquidity management, regulatory capital), sovereign wealth funds (long-term national wealth management). Each type has distinct return requirements, risk tolerance, time horizon, liquidity needs, tax status, and regulatory considerations that shape portfolio strategy.

LOS 3.d Coming Soon
Describe defined contribution and defined benefit pension plans
Compare pension plan structures. Defined Benefit (DB) plans: employer promises specific retirement benefits based on salary and years of service; employer bears investment risk; professional management of pooled assets; focuses on funding liabilities; uses actuarial assumptions; employee has predictable retirement income. Defined Contribution (DC) plans: employer and/or employee contribute specified amounts; employee bears investment risk; individual accounts with participant-directed investments; retirement income depends on contributions and investment performance; portability when changing jobs. DB plans are declining due to cost and risk for employers, while DC plans (like 401(k)s) are increasingly common, shifting investment responsibility and risk to employees.

LOS 3.e Coming Soon
Describe aspects of the asset management industry
Explore the asset management landscape. Key participants: investment managers (active vs. passive strategies), financial advisors (client-facing wealth management), broker-dealers (transaction execution, research), custodians (safekeeping of assets, settlement), consultants (manager selection, performance evaluation). Industry trends include: growth of passive investing and index funds, fee compression, robo-advisors and technology adoption, increased focus on ESG factors, regulatory oversight (fiduciary standards), alternative investments becoming more accessible, and asset consolidation among large managers. The industry serves both institutional and retail clients across diverse strategies, asset classes, and geographical markets.

Describe mutual funds and compare them with other pooled investment products
Understand pooled investment vehicles. Mutual funds: open-end funds that issue and redeem shares at NAV, daily liquidity, regulated (e.g., '40 Act), typically lower fees, professional management, diversification. Compare with: Exchange-Traded Funds (ETFs)‚Äîtrade on exchanges, intraday liquidity, often passive/indexed, tax efficient; Closed-End Funds‚Äîfixed number of shares, trade at premium/discount to NAV, less liquid; Hedge Funds‚Äîlimited regulation, accredited investors only, sophisticated strategies, higher fees (2-and-20), lock-ups; Private Equity Funds‚Äîilliquid, long commitment periods, direct ownership stakes; Separately Managed Accounts (SMAs)‚Äîindividual portfolios, customization, direct ownership. Each structure offers different liquidity, fees, tax treatment, and regulatory oversight.
