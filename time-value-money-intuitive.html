Time Value of Money in Fixed Income & Equity

Understanding how money's value changes over time - explained simply!

üéØ Let's Start With a Simple Question

Would you rather have ‚Çπ10,000 today or ‚Çπ10,000 one year from now?

If you're like most people, you'd choose the money today! But why? üí°

Here's the thing: money you have right now can be invested to grow. If you put ‚Çπ10,000 in a fixed deposit today at 7% interest, you'd have ‚Çπ10,700 in a year. So ‚Çπ10,000 today is actually worth more than ‚Çπ10,000 in the future!

This fundamental idea - that money available today is worth more than the same amount in the future - is called the Time Value of Money. It's the foundation for understanding bonds, stocks, loans, and basically every investment decision you'll ever make! üöÄ

üí∞ The Magic Formula: Present Value and Future Value

üé™ Think of Money Like a Plant üå±

Imagine you plant a seed (your money today). Over time, with the right conditions (interest rate), it grows into a tree (future value). The formula tells us exactly how big that tree will be!

The Growth Formula (Future Value)

FV = PV √ó (1 + r)·µó

What does this mean in plain English?

  • FV = Future Value (how much you'll have later)
  • PV = Present Value (how much you have now)
  • r = Interest rate per period (the growth rate)
  • t = Number of time periods (how long you wait)

üîç Let's Break Down the Algebra (Super Simply!)

Why do we multiply by (1 + r)?

Think about it this way: If you have ‚Çπ100 and earn 10% interest:

  • You keep your original ‚Çπ100 ‚Üí that's the "1"
  • You earn 10% more ‚Üí that's the "r" (0.10)
  • Together you have 1.10 times your money ‚Üí (1 + 0.10) = 1.10

Why the power of t?

The "t" represents compounding - earning interest on your interest!

  • Year 1: You multiply by (1 + r) once
  • Year 2: You multiply by (1 + r) again on the NEW amount
  • Year 3: You do it again!
  • So for 3 years: (1 + r) √ó (1 + r) √ó (1 + r) = (1 + r)¬≥

The reverse formula (Present Value):

If FV = PV √ó (1 + r)·µó, then to find PV, we just divide both sides:

PV = FV √∑ (1 + r)·µó or PV = FV √ó (1 + r)‚Åª·µó

This is called discounting - we're finding what future money is worth today!

Visual representation of how money grows over time with compound interest

üìä Fixed-Income Instruments: Understanding Bonds

What's a bond anyway? üí°

Think of a bond as an IOU. When you buy a bond, you're lending money to a government or company. They promise to:

  • Pay you back the principal (face value) at maturity
  • Pay you interest (coupon payments) along the way

The "yield-to-maturity" (YTM) is the total return you'll earn if you hold the bond until it matures. It's like the interest rate for the bond!

üéØ Type 1: Discount Bonds (Zero-Coupon Bonds)

üéÅ Think of it Like a Gift Card

Imagine buying a ‚Çπ1,000 gift card for ‚Çπ800. You pay less today and get the full value later. That discount (‚Çπ200) is essentially your interest! No monthly payments - just one lump sum at the end.

Discount bond: You pay less today and receive full value at maturity

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example: Government Bond Strip

Scenario: The Reserve Bank of India offers a 20-year government bond strip (zero-coupon) with a yield of 6.70%. What price should you pay today for ‚Çπ100 face value?

Solution:

PV = ‚Çπ100 √∑ (1 + 0.067)¬≤‚Å∞

Calculating:

  • (1.067)¬≤‚Å∞ = 3.66
  • PV = ‚Çπ100 √∑ 3.66
  • PV = ‚Çπ27.33

Answer: You should pay ‚Çπ27.33 today to receive ‚Çπ100 in 20 years. The ‚Çπ72.67 difference is your interest earned over 20 years!

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Insight: Notice how you're paying much less than face value. This is why they're called "discount" bonds - you buy them at a discount!

üí∏ What Happens When Interest Rates Change?

Here's a key principle: Bond prices and interest rates move in opposite directions! üîÑ

Think about it this way:

  • If interest rates go UP ‚Üí New bonds offer better returns ‚Üí Your old bond becomes less attractive ‚Üí Price goes DOWN
  • If interest rates go DOWN ‚Üí New bonds offer worse returns ‚Üí Your old bond becomes more attractive ‚Üí Price goes UP

üìâ Seeing the Inverse Relationship

Continuing the Indian example: What if interest rates suddenly jump to 7.70%? What's the new bond price?

Solution:

PV = ‚Çπ100 √∑ (1 + 0.077)¬≤‚Å∞ = ‚Çπ100 √∑ 4.41 = ‚Çπ22.68

Result: Price dropped from ‚Çπ27.33 to ‚Çπ22.68 - that's a loss of ‚Çπ4.65!

When interest rates went up by 1%, the bond price fell. This is the inverse relationship in action! üìä

üéà Type 2: Coupon Bonds (Regular Interest Payments)

üè† Think of it Like Rental Income

Imagine owning a rental property. Every month, you receive rent (like coupon payments). At the end, you also get the property back (like receiving the principal). A coupon bond works the same way - regular income plus your money back at the end!

Coupon bond: Regular interest payments plus principal at maturity

Pricing a Coupon Bond

To find the present value, we discount each cash flow:

PV = (Coupon‚ÇÅ)/(1+r)¬π + (Coupon‚ÇÇ)/(1+r)¬≤ + ... + (Coupon‚Çô + Principal)/(1+r)‚Åø

Simplified explanation:

  • Take each future cash flow
  • Discount it back to today using the formula
  • Add them all up!

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example: Semiannual Coupon Bond

Scenario: Republic of India issues a 20-year bond with 6.70% annual coupon paid semiannually. If YTM is 6.70%, what's the price?

Key insight: When coupon rate equals YTM, the bond trades at par (‚Çπ100)!

But let's verify with calculation:

  • Semiannual coupon = ‚Çπ6.70/2 = ‚Çπ3.35
  • Semiannual rate = 6.70%/2 = 3.35%
  • Number of periods = 20 years √ó 2 = 40 periods
PV = ‚Çπ3.35/(1.0335)¬π + ‚Çπ3.35/(1.0335)¬≤ + ... + ‚Çπ103.35/(1.0335)‚Å¥‚Å∞

Result: PV = ‚Çπ100 exactly!

üí° Remember This Rule:

  • Coupon Rate = YTM ‚Üí Bond trades at Par (‚Çπ100)
  • Coupon Rate > YTM ‚Üí Bond trades at Premium (>‚Çπ100)
  • Coupon Rate < YTM ‚Üí Bond trades at Discount (<‚Çπ100)

‚ôæÔ∏è Type 3: Perpetual Bonds (Forever Payments!)

üéº Think of it Like Royalty Payments

Imagine you wrote a hit song that plays on the radio forever. You get royalties every quarter, forever! That's like a perpetual bond - it never matures, so you keep getting paid indefinitely.

The Perpetuity Formula (Super Simple!)

PV = Payment √∑ Interest Rate

Why does this work?

When payments go on forever, the complicated discounting formula simplifies beautifully! If you get ‚Çπ100 every year at 10% interest, you just divide: ‚Çπ100 √∑ 0.10 = ‚Çπ1,000

üåç Global Example: KB Financial Perpetual Bond

Scenario: KB Financial issued a perpetual bond with 3.30% annual coupon (paid quarterly). The bond price is KRW 97.03 per KRW 100 face value. What's the YTM?

Solution:

  • Quarterly payment = 3.30% √ó KRW 100 √∑ 4 = KRW 0.825
  • Using the formula: PV = Payment √∑ r
  • KRW 97.03 = KRW 0.825 √∑ r
  • r = KRW 0.825 √∑ KRW 97.03 = 0.85% per quarter
  • Annual YTM = 0.85% √ó 4 = 3.40%

Insight: Notice the bond trades below par (97.03 < 100). This makes sense because the coupon rate (3.30%) is less than the YTM (3.40%)!

üè† Annuities: Level Payment Instruments (Like a Home Loan!)

What's an annuity? üí≠

Think of your home loan or car loan. Every month, you pay the same amount - part goes to interest, part reduces the principal. That's an annuity - a series of equal payments over time!

As time passes, less of your payment goes to interest and more goes to principal!

The Annuity Payment Formula

To calculate the equal periodic payment:

Payment = [r √ó PV] √∑ [1 - (1 + r)‚Åª·µó]

Breaking it down:

  • r = interest rate per period
  • PV = present value (loan amount)
  • t = total number of payments

The numerator (r √ó PV): This is how much interest you'd owe in one period

The denominator [1 - (1 + r)‚Åª·µó]: This adjusts for the fact that you're making multiple payments over time

üìà Equity Instruments: Valuing Stocks

How are stocks different from bonds? ü§î

With bonds, you know exactly what you'll get - fixed interest and your principal back. But stocks are different:

  • No maturity date - they can last forever!
  • Dividends are not guaranteed - companies pay them when they want
  • The company can grow (or shrink), affecting stock price

So how do we value something with so much uncertainty? We use dividend discount models! üí°

üíé Type 1: Constant Dividends (Like Preferred Stock)

üé∞ Think of it Like a Slot Machine... That Always Pays!

Imagine a slot machine that pays you ‚Çπ150 every year, forever. How much would you pay for this machine? Well, if you want a 15% return, you'd pay ‚Çπ1,000 (because ‚Çπ150 √∑ 0.15 = ‚Çπ1,000). That's exactly how we value stocks with constant dividends!

Constant Dividend Formula (Perpetuity Again!)

Stock Price = Dividend √∑ Required Return

This is the same perpetuity formula we saw with perpetual bonds!

üåç Global Example: Shipline PLC

Scenario: Shipline PLC pays ¬£1.50 dividend annually, expected to continue forever. If investors require a 15% return, what's the stock price?

Solution:

Price = ¬£1.50 √∑ 0.15 = ¬£10.00

Answer: The stock should trade at ¬£10 per share.

üå± Type 2: Constant Growth (Gordon Growth Model)

üå≥ Think of it Like a Fruit Tree

Imagine a mango tree that produces 100 mangoes this year. But it's still growing! Next year it gives 106 mangoes (6% growth), then 112 the year after. If this growth continues forever, how much is the tree worth? That's the Gordon Growth Model for stocks!

Dividends growing at a constant rate over time

The Gordon Growth Model

Stock Price = D‚ÇÅ √∑ (r - g)

Where:

  • D‚ÇÅ = Next year's expected dividend = D‚ÇÄ √ó (1 + g)
  • r = Required rate of return
  • g = Constant growth rate

Why (r - g)? Think of it this way: if a company grows at 6% but you want 15% return, the "net" discount rate is 9% (15% - 6%). The growth partially offsets the discounting!

üåç Continuing Shipline PLC Example

Scenario: What if Shipline's ¬£1.50 dividend grows at 6% annually instead of staying constant? Required return is still 15%.

Solution:

  • Current dividend (D‚ÇÄ) = ¬£1.50
  • Next dividend (D‚ÇÅ) = ¬£1.50 √ó (1.06) = ¬£1.59
  • Required return (r) = 15%
  • Growth rate (g) = 6%
Price = ¬£1.59 √∑ (0.15 - 0.06) = ¬£1.59 √∑ 0.09 = ¬£17.67

Comparison:

  • With NO growth: ¬£10.00
  • With 6% growth: ¬£17.67

Insight: The growth assumption makes a HUGE difference in valuation! Growth is worth ¬£7.67 more per share. üöÄ

üöÄ Type 3: Two-Stage Growth (Reality Check!)

Here's the reality: Companies don't grow at the same rate forever! üìä

Think about a startup: it might grow super fast initially (30-40% per year), but as it matures, growth slows down to more sustainable levels (5-7%). The two-stage model captures this reality!

üéì Key Takeaways

Remember These Core Concepts:

  1. Time Value of Money: Money today is worth more than money tomorrow because it can be invested to grow
  2. Present vs Future Value: FV = PV √ó (1+r)·µó goes forward; PV = FV √∑ (1+r)·µó discounts back
  3. Discount Bonds: One payment at maturity - the discount is your interest
  4. Coupon Bonds: Regular interest + principal at end - price moves opposite to rates
  5. Perpetual Bonds: Forever payments - just divide by the rate
  6. Annuities: Equal payments over time - like a mortgage
  7. Stock Valuation: Based on expected dividends discounted to present
  8. Growth Matters: Higher growth = higher stock price

üéØ The Big Picture

All these concepts - bonds, stocks, loans, annuities - they all use the same fundamental principle: the time value of money. Whether you're pricing a government bond, valuing a company's stock, or calculating your home loan EMI, you're always asking the same question:

"What are future cash flows worth today?"

Master this concept, and you've unlocked the foundation of finance! üîë
