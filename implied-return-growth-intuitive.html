The Core Idea: Working Backwards from Price üîô

Normally, when you know the return rate, you can calculate what something is worth today. But what if you already know today's price and want to figure out what return everyone is expecting? That's exactly what implied return means!

üéÇ The Birthday Cake Analogy

Think of it like reverse-engineering a recipe:

Normal Way: You have a recipe (return rate) ‚Üí You bake the cake (calculate price)

Implied Return Way: You taste the finished cake (see the price) ‚Üí You figure out the recipe (solve for return)

If someone paid ‚Çπ500 for a cake that usually costs ‚Çπ400, you can deduce they expect it to be extra special (higher implied quality/return)!

Part 1: Implied Return for Bonds (Fixed Income) üí∞

Let's start with bonds because they're simpler - you know exactly what cash flows you'll get!

Type 1: Discount Bonds (Zero-Coupon Bonds)

üéØ The Simple Case

A discount bond is like a promise: "Pay me ‚Çπ95 today, and I'll give you ‚Çπ100 in 4 years." No interest payments in between - just one payment at the end.

The question: What annual return are you earning by accepting this deal?

The Magic Formula for Discount Bonds

r = (FV / PV)1/t - 1

Where:

‚Ä¢ r = implied return (what we're solving for)

‚Ä¢ FV = future value (what you'll receive)

‚Ä¢ PV = present value (what you pay today)

‚Ä¢ t = time period in years

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example: Government Bond

Situation: You can buy an RBI bond today for ‚Çπ95.72. It will pay you exactly ‚Çπ100 in 4 years. No coupon payments.

Question: What's your implied annual return?

Let's solve step by step:

1. What we know: PV = ‚Çπ95.72, FV = ‚Çπ100, t = 4 years

2. Plug into formula: r = (100 / 95.72)1/4 - 1

3. Calculate: r = (1.0447)0.25 - 1

4. Result: r = 1.0110 - 1 = 0.0110 = 1.10%

üí° What this means: The market is pricing this bond to give you a 1.10% annual return. If you think you can get better than 1.10% elsewhere, don't buy this bond!

üåç Global Example: German Government Bond Surprise

The Weird Story: In 2016, Germany issued a 10-year bond at ‚Ç¨100.50 (yes, you paid MORE than face value!). Six years later, it was trading at ‚Ç¨95.72.

What was the investor's actual return over those 6 years?

‚Ä¢ Started with: ‚Ç¨100.50 (what they paid)

‚Ä¢ Ended with: ‚Ç¨95.72 (current value)

‚Ä¢ Time: 6 years

Using our formula: r = (95.72 / 100.50)1/6 - 1 = -0.81%

ü§Ø Mind-blowing insight: They LOST money every year! This happened because inflation rose in Europe, making the bond less attractive. Remember: bond prices fall when interest rates rise!

Type 2: Coupon Bonds (Bonds with Periodic Interest)

üéØ The More Realistic Case

Most bonds don't just pay you at the end - they pay you interest every 6 months or year. This is like a rental property that gives you rent checks regularly, plus you get the property back at the end!

Calculating implied return here is trickier because you need to account for reinvesting those interest payments at the same rate.

üè† The Rental Property Analogy

Scenario: You buy a rental property for ‚Çπ30 lakhs. Every year you get ‚Çπ60,000 in rent. After 5 years, you sell it back for ‚Çπ30 lakhs.

Simple Question: What's your return?

Complication: Each year's rent can be reinvested! If you reinvest at 5%, your final value is different than if you stuff cash under your mattress. The implied return calculation assumes you reinvest everything at the same rate!

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example: Greek Bond Bought by an NRI

Setup: In 2020, a 7-year Greek government bond was issued with a 2% annual coupon. By 2022, it was trading at ‚Ç¨93.091 per ‚Ç¨100 face value.

Question: What was the 2-year return for someone who bought at issue and held for 2 years?

Step 1: Calculate what you have after 2 years

‚Ä¢ Year 1 coupon: ‚Ç¨2 (reinvested at 2% for 1 year) = ‚Ç¨2 √ó 1.02 = ‚Ç¨2.04

‚Ä¢ Year 2 coupon: ‚Ç¨2 (just received, no time to reinvest)

‚Ä¢ Current bond value: ‚Ç¨93.091

‚Ä¢ Total FV after 2 years: ‚Ç¨97.13

Step 2: Calculate return

r = (97.13 / 100)1/2 - 1 = -1.445% per year

üòü Ouch! Despite getting interest payments, the investor lost money because the bond price dropped by ‚Ç¨6.91, which was more than the ‚Ç¨4.04 earned from coupons!

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Insight: YTM vs. Realized Return

Yield to Maturity (YTM) is the implied return IF you hold the bond until maturity AND can reinvest all coupons at that same YTM.

But in real life:

‚Ä¢ You might sell early (like our example above)

‚Ä¢ Reinvestment rates might be different

‚Ä¢ Bond prices change as interest rates change

Bottom line: YTM shows market expectations, but your actual return depends on timing and luck!

Part 2: Implied Return and Growth for Stocks (Equity) üìà

Now things get interesting! With stocks, you're not just solving for return - you're solving for BOTH return AND growth, because they're connected!

üå≥ The Growing Mango Tree Analogy

Think of a stock like owning a mango tree:

‚Ä¢ The tree gives you mangoes each year (dividends)

‚Ä¢ The tree grows bigger over time, producing more mangoes (dividend growth)

‚Ä¢ You can sell the tree later (capital gains)

If someone pays ‚Çπ50,000 for a tree that gives ‚Çπ2,000 worth of mangoes this year, what are they assuming? They're betting on:

1. Return (r): Their total return including dividends and price appreciation

2. Growth (g): How fast the mango production (dividends) grows each year

These two are tied together - you can't solve for one without assuming the other!

The Gordon Growth Model: Your Stock Pricing Secret Weapon

Two Ways to Look at It:

Finding Required Return:

r = (D‚ÇÅ / P‚ÇÄ) + g

Where: D‚ÇÅ = next year's dividend, P‚ÇÄ = current price, g = growth rate

Finding Implied Growth:

g = r - (D‚ÇÅ / P‚ÇÄ)

Same formula, just rearranged!

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example: Reliance Industries

Scenario: Reliance stock trades at ‚Çπ2,500. It's expected to pay ‚Çπ20 per share in dividends next year.

Question 1: If you assume dividends will grow at 6% forever, what return are investors expecting?

‚Ä¢ Dividend Yield = ‚Çπ20 / ‚Çπ2,500 = 0.008 = 0.8%

‚Ä¢ Growth Rate = 6%

‚Ä¢ Required Return = 0.8% + 6% = 6.8%

Question 2: What if you believe investors should expect an 8% return? What growth is implied?

‚Ä¢ g = 8% - 0.8% = 7.2%

üí° Interpretation: If you think 7.2% growth is too optimistic (maybe Reliance can only grow at 5%), then the stock is overpriced at ‚Çπ2,500!

üåç Global Example: Coca-Cola Stock

Given:

‚Ä¢ Stock Price: $63.00

‚Ä¢ Expected Dividend (next year): $1.76

‚Ä¢ Expected Dividend Growth: 4% per year forever

Calculate the required return:

r = ($1.76 / $63.00) + 0.04

r = 0.0279 + 0.04 = 0.0679 = 6.79%

What this tells us: At $63, Coca-Cola stock is priced to deliver a 6.79% return if dividends grow at 4% forever. If you think:

‚Ä¢ 6.79% is too low ‚Üí Don't buy (overpriced)

‚Ä¢ 6.79% is attractive ‚Üí Buy! (good deal)

‚Ä¢ 4% growth is too optimistic ‚Üí Stock is overpriced

‚Ä¢ 4% growth is too pessimistic ‚Üí Stock is underpriced

The Price-to-Earnings Ratio: Another Window into Returns üìä

Here's where things get super practical! Instead of looking at prices in dollars, investors often compare using the Price-to-Earnings (P/E) ratio.

üéØ What is P/E Ratio?

Think of P/E ratio like asking: "How many years of earnings am I paying for?"

If Infosys has earnings of ‚Çπ100 per share and trades at ‚Çπ2,000:

P/E = ‚Çπ2,000 / ‚Çπ100 = 20

This means you're paying 20 times annual earnings. Is that expensive? Cheap? It depends on growth expectations!

The P/E Formula Connection

P/E = (Dividend Payout Ratio) / (r - g)

This reveals three insights:

1. Higher dividend payout ‚Üí Higher P/E (more generous to shareholders)

2. Higher growth (g) ‚Üí Higher P/E (more valuable future)

3. Higher required return (r) ‚Üí Lower P/E (investors demand more, pay less)

üáÆüá≥ Indian Example: TCS P/E Analysis

Scenario: TCS stock has a P/E ratio of 25, and pays out 60% of earnings as dividends.

Question 1: If analysts believe TCS can grow at 5% and should deliver 9% returns, is TCS fairly priced?

Let's check: P/E = 0.60 / (0.09 - 0.05) = 0.60 / 0.04 = 15

Actual P/E is 25, but fair value is 15!

Conclusion: TCS looks overvalued. Either:

‚Ä¢ The market expects higher growth (more than 5%)

‚Ä¢ The market expects lower returns (less than 9%)

‚Ä¢ Investors are paying too much!

Question 2: What growth rate is implied by the P/E of 25?

25 = 0.60 / (0.09 - g)

0.09 - g = 0.60 / 25 = 0.024

g = 0.09 - 0.024 = 6.6%

Interpretation: The market is pricing TCS as if it will grow at 6.6% forever. If you think that's realistic, the P/E of 25 makes sense!

üåç Global Example: The Entire S&P 500!

Yes, we can analyze entire stock indexes the same way!

Given:

‚Ä¢ S&P 500 P/E Ratio: 19

‚Ä¢ Average Dividend Payout: 60%

‚Ä¢ Investors expect 8% returns

What's the implied growth rate for the entire US stock market?

19 = 0.60 / (0.08 - g)

0.08 - g = 0.60 / 19 = 0.0316

g = 0.08 - 0.0316 = 4.84%

üí° Big Picture Insight: The US stock market is priced for 4.84% dividend growth. This seems reasonable given historical US GDP growth of 3-4% plus inflation. If growth slows below this, stocks could fall!

üß† The Critical Insight: Returns and Growth are Married

Here's the mind-bending part: You can't separate expected returns from expected growth in stock prices.

A stock with P/E = 28 and 70% payout tells us that (r - g) = 2.5%

This could mean:

‚Ä¢ 6.5% return with 4% growth, OR

‚Ä¢ 9% return with 6.5% growth, OR

‚Ä¢ 5% return with 2.5% growth

Your job as an analyst: Estimate BOTH return expectations AND growth expectations to see if the current price makes sense!

Key Takeaways: The Treasure Map üó∫Ô∏è

üìö Remember These Core Ideas

1. Implied return means "working backwards"

You know the price and future cash flows, so you solve for the return that makes them equal.

2. For discount bonds, it's straightforward

r = (FV / PV)^(1/t) - 1. One formula, done!

3. For coupon bonds, account for reinvestment

Add up all cash flows with their reinvestment, then solve. Or use the YTM (which assumes you hold to maturity).

4. For stocks, you need both return AND growth

r = (Dividend Yield) + g. They're connected - you can't have one without the other!

5. P/E ratios encode return-growth expectations

High P/E = expecting high growth or accepting low returns. Low P/E = expecting low growth or demanding high returns.

6. Your advantage: Compare market expectations to YOUR expectations

If the market expects 4% growth but you think it'll be 6% ‚Üí Stock is underpriced! üí∞
