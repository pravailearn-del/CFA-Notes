Detailed Notes

Money-Weighted and Time-Weighted Rates of Return

Learning Outcome Statement

Compare the money-weighted and time-weighted rates of return and evaluate the performance of portfolios based on these measures.

1. Introduction and Context

The arithmetic and geometric return computations discussed in prior learning objectives do not account for the timing of cash flows into and out of a portfolio. When investors make contributions to or withdrawals from a portfolio during the measurement period, two distinct methodologies are employed to calculate returns:

  1. Money-Weighted Rate of Return (MWRR) - Accounts for the timing and magnitude of cash flows
  2. Time-Weighted Rate of Return (TWRR) - Eliminates the effect of cash flow timing

The choice between these methods depends on the purpose of the performance measurement and whether the portfolio manager has control over the timing of cash flows.

2. Money-Weighted Rate of Return

Definition

The money-weighted rate of return is a measure of the rate of growth of all funds invested in an account over a stated measurement period. It accounts for the timing and magnitude of all cash flows and provides the investor with information on the actual dollar-weighted return earned on the investment.

2.1 Mathematical Framework

The money-weighted return is calculated using the internal rate of return (IRR) methodology. The IRR is the discount rate that equates the present value of all cash inflows to the present value of all cash outflows.

General IRR Equation

Σ [CFₜ / (1 + IRR)ᵗ] = 0

Where:

  • CFₜ = Cash flow at time t (negative for outflows, positive for inflows)
  • IRR = Internal rate of return (the money-weighted rate of return)
  • t = Time period (0, 1, 2, ..., T)
  • T = Total number of periods

Cash Flow Sign Convention

Negative cash flows (-): Represent money invested (outflows from investor's perspective)

Positive cash flows (+): Represent money received, withdrawn, or remaining value (inflows to investor)

2.2 Detailed Example: Portfolio with Multiple Cash Flows

Example 1: Three-Year Mutual Fund Investment

An investor makes the following transactions in a mutual fund:

Year Beginning Balance New Investment Withdrawal Net Balance Annual Return Investment Gain/Loss Ending Balance
1 €0 €100 €0 €100 -50% -€50 €50
2 €50 €950 €0 €1,000 +35% +€350 €1,350
3 €1,350 €0 -€350 €1,000 +27% +€270 €1,270

Cash Flow Timeline

Converting the above information to cash flows from the investor's perspective:

  • CF₀ = -€100 (initial investment at end of Year 0/beginning of Year 1)
  • CF₁ = -€950 (additional investment at end of Year 1/beginning of Year 2)
  • CF₂ = +€350 (withdrawal at end of Year 2)
  • CF₃ = +€1,270 (final portfolio value at end of Year 3)

Calculation

The money-weighted return equation becomes:

-100/(1+IRR)⁰ + (-950)/(1+IRR)¹ + 350/(1+IRR)² + 1,270/(1+IRR)³ = 0

Solving for IRR (using financial calculator or spreadsheet):

Money-Weighted Return = 26.11% per annum

Interpretation

The 26.11% return reflects the investor's actual annualized return accounting for the specific timing and amounts invested. This return is significantly higher than the arithmetic mean (4%) or geometric mean (2.66%) of the annual returns because:

  • Only €100 was exposed to the -50% return in Year 1
  • The majority of capital (€950) was invested at the start of Year 2, capturing the +35% and +27% returns

2.3 Example: Dividend-Paying Stock

Example 2: Stock Investment with Dividends

An investor purchases shares over a two-year period:

Time Transaction Cash Flow
t = 0 Purchase 1 share at $200 -$200
t = 1 Receive dividend ($5) on first share +$5
t = 1 Purchase 1 additional share at $225 -$225
t = 1 (net) Net cash flow at t = 1 -$220
t = 2 Receive dividends ($5 × 2 shares) +$10
t = 2 Sell 2 shares at $235 each +$470
t = 2 (net) Net cash flow at t = 2 +$480

IRR Calculation

-200/(1+IRR)⁰ + (-220)/(1+IRR)¹ + 480/(1+IRR)² = 0

Money-Weighted Return = 9.39%

Period-by-Period Analysis

Year 1 Holding Period Return:

HPR₁ = ($5 + $225 - $200) / $200 = 15%

Year 2 Holding Period Return:

Beginning value: $225 × 2 shares = $450

HPR₂ = ($10 + $470 - $450) / $450 = 6.67%

Arithmetic Mean: (15% + 6.67%) / 2 = 10.84%

The money-weighted return of 9.39% is lower than the arithmetic mean because more capital was invested in Year 2 when performance was weaker (6.67%) compared to Year 1 (15%).

2.4 Limitations of Money-Weighted Returns

Key Limitations

  1. Lack of comparability: Two investors in the same fund with different cash flow patterns will have different money-weighted returns
  2. Timing dependency: Returns are heavily influenced by investor behavior (contribution/withdrawal timing), which may not reflect manager skill
  3. External factors: Captures effects of decisions outside the portfolio manager's control

Appropriate use: Measuring the actual dollar-weighted return experienced by a specific investor

3. Time-Weighted Rate of Return

Definition

The time-weighted rate of return measures the compound rate of growth of $1 (or €1) initially invested in the portfolio over a stated measurement period. It is independent of cash flows and provides a pure measure of investment performance.

3.1 Calculation Methodology

The time-weighted rate of return is calculated using a three-step process:

  1. Break the evaluation period into subperiods: Divide the measurement period based on the dates of significant cash inflows or outflows. Value the portfolio immediately before each cash flow.
  2. Calculate holding period returns: Compute the return for each subperiod using the standard holding period return formula.
  3. Link the subperiod returns: Chain-link or compound the individual returns to obtain the time-weighted return for the entire period.

Time-Weighted Return Formula

For a single year with multiple subperiods:

RTW = [(1 + R₁) × (1 + R₂) × ... × (1 + Rₙ)] - 1

For multiple years, annualize using geometric mean:

RTW = [(1 + R₁) × (1 + R₂) × ... × (1 + Rₙ)]^(1/N) - 1

Where:

  • Rᵢ = Holding period return for subperiod i
  • N = Number of years (for annualization)

3.2 Detailed Example: Two-Year Stock Investment

Example 3: Calculating Time-Weighted Return

Using the same stock investment from Example 2, calculate the time-weighted return.

Given Information:

  • Year 1 HPR = 15%
  • Year 2 HPR = 6.67%

Step 1: Identify Subperiods

The investment period consists of two one-year subperiods.

Step 2: Calculate Returns

Already calculated: R₁ = 15% and R₂ = 6.67%

Step 3: Link Returns

RTW = [(1 + 0.15) × (1 + 0.0667)]^(1/2) - 1
RTW = [1.15 × 1.0667]^0.5 - 1
RTW = 1.2267^0.5 - 1
RTW = 1.1076 - 1
RTW = 0.1076 or 10.76%

Comparison

Measure Value Interpretation
Money-Weighted Return 9.39% Actual investor return with cash flow timing
Time-Weighted Return 10.76% Pure portfolio performance measure
Arithmetic Mean 10.84% Simple average of period returns

The time-weighted return (10.76%) exceeds the money-weighted return (9.39%) because it does not give extra weight to Year 2's lower performance when more capital was invested.

3.3 Practical Implementation

Portfolio Valuation Frequency

For exact time-weighted returns, portfolios should be valued immediately prior to each significant cash flow. However, this can be costly with frequent transactions.

Common Practice: Value portfolios at regular intervals (typically daily) to obtain approximate time-weighted returns. The approximation improves with:

  • More frequent valuations
  • Cash flows that occur at regular interval endpoints
  • Cash flows uncorrelated with market movements

4. Comparative Analysis

4.1 Selection Criteria

Evaluation Purpose Recommended Method Rationale
Portfolio manager evaluation Time-Weighted Return Isolates investment decisions from cash flow decisions that managers don't control
Individual investor performance Money-Weighted Return Reflects actual dollar-weighted returns based on investor's cash flow timing
Fund marketing/comparison Time-Weighted Return Provides standardized, comparable performance metrics across funds
Assessing investment timing skill Both (Comparison) Difference reveals impact of cash flow timing decisions

4.2 Interpretation of Differences

When Money-Weighted > Time-Weighted

The investor exhibited favorable timing, contributing more capital during periods of strong performance or withdrawing capital before periods of weak performance.

When Time-Weighted > Money-Weighted

The investor exhibited unfavorable timing, contributing more capital during periods of weak performance or withdrawing capital before periods of strong performance.

Note: Neither outcome implies superior or inferior manager performance. The difference reflects only the interaction between cash flow timing and returns.

5. Regulatory and Industry Standards

Performance Presentation Standards

Global Investment Performance Standards (GIPS®): Require the use of time-weighted returns for performance presentation to ensure fair and comparable disclosure of investment results.

Rationale: Time-weighted returns provide the most meaningful basis for comparing the performance of different investment managers or funds since they are not influenced by client-directed cash flows.

6. Summary and Exam Tips

Key Takeaways

  1. Money-weighted return = IRR = Actual investor return with cash flow timing
  2. Time-weighted return = Geometric mean = Pure portfolio performance
  3. Use time-weighted for manager evaluation (preferred method)
  4. Use money-weighted for actual investor return calculation
  5. Differences between the two measures reveal cash flow timing effects
  6. Time-weighted returns require portfolio valuation at each significant cash flow
  7. Both measures are correct; they answer different questions

Exam Problem-Solving Strategy

  1. Identify the measure required: Read the question carefully to determine whether money-weighted or time-weighted return is requested
  2. For money-weighted returns:
    • List all cash flows with proper signs
    • Identify time periods
    • Use IRR function or solve IRR equation
  3. For time-weighted returns:
    • Break into subperiods based on cash flows
    • Calculate holding period return for each subperiod
    • Chain-link returns using geometric compounding
  4. Interpret results: Be prepared to explain why the two measures differ and what the difference signifies about cash flow timing
