Return Measurement Approaches

Learning Outcome Statement

Calculate and interpret different approaches to return measurement over time and describe their appropriate uses.

1. Introduction to Return Measurement

Financial assets are characterized primarily by their return and risk profiles. Understanding various return measurement methodologies is essential for portfolio construction, performance evaluation, and comparative analysis.

1.1 Sources of Return

Financial assets typically generate returns through two distinct mechanisms:

Periodic Income

Cash flows received during the holding period, including:

  • Cash dividends from equity securities
  • Interest payments from fixed-income instruments
  • Coupon payments from bonds

Capital Appreciation/Depreciation

Changes in the market value of the asset:

  • Capital gains from price increases
  • Capital losses from price decreases

Some assets provide returns exclusively through one mechanism (e.g., non-dividend-paying growth stocks rely solely on capital appreciation), while others generate returns through both channels.

6. Comparative Analysis and Selection Criteria

Decision Framework for Return Measure Selection

Use Arithmetic Mean When:

  • Forecasting expected return for the next single period
  • Estimating average one-period performance
  • Statistical properties (e.g., standard deviation) are required

Use Geometric Mean When:

  • Reporting historical investment performance
  • Calculating compound annual growth rates (CAGR)
  • Comparing multi-period investment returns
  • Determining the actual growth rate of wealth over time

Use Harmonic Mean When:

  • Analyzing cost averaging strategies
  • Computing average prices with fixed monetary investments
  • Evaluating periodic investment plans (SIP, dollar-cost averaging)
  • Averaging price-to-earnings ratios or other valuation multiples
