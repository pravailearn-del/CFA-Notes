Understanding Business Cycles

Module Overview: This module examines business cycle phases, credit cycles, and economic indicators. Understanding how different sectors and activities vary across expansion and contraction phases.

Learning Outcome Statements (LOS)

LOS 2.a Coming Soon
Describe the business cycle and its phases
Understand the four phases of the business cycle: expansion (rising GDP, employment, and confidence), peak (maximum output before downturn), contraction/recession (declining GDP, rising unemployment), and trough (lowest point before recovery). Analyze characteristics of each phase including changes in production, employment, consumer spending, and business investment. Identify turning points (peaks and troughs) and their significance for investment decisions.

LOS 2.b Coming Soon
Describe credit cycles
Examine credit cycles and their relationship with business cycles. Understand expansion phases with easy credit availability, low default rates, rising asset prices, and increased leverage. Analyze contraction phases with tightening credit conditions, rising defaults, falling asset prices, and deleveraging. Explore the role of financial institutions, credit spreads, and lending standards in amplifying business cycle fluctuations through the financial accelerator mechanism.

LOS 2.c Coming Soon
Describe how resource use, consumer and business activity, housing sector activity, and external trade sector activity vary over the business cycle and describe their measurement using economic indicators
Analyze cyclical behavior of key economic sectors. Resource use: capacity utilization and employment rates rise in expansions. Consumer activity: retail sales and consumer confidence peak at cycle tops. Business activity: capital expenditures and inventory levels vary with outlook. Housing: construction starts and home sales lead the cycle. External trade: imports rise faster than exports in expansions. Learn economic indicators: leading (stock prices, building permits, yield curve), coincident (GDP, employment, industrial production), and lagging (unemployment duration, inventory-to-sales ratio, unit labor costs).
