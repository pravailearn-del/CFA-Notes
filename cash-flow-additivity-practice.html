Question 1 Easy
Which of the following best describes the cash flow additivity principle?
A The present value of a series of cash flows equals the sum of the present values of each individual cash flow.
B Future cash flows can only be added together if they occur at the same point in time.
C The sum of cash outflows must equal the sum of cash inflows to prevent arbitrage.

‚úì Correct Answer: A

Explanation: The cash flow additivity principle states that the present value of any stream of cash flows equals the sum of the present values of the individual cash flows. This is a fundamental property that enables decomposition and recombination of cash flows without changing their total present value.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • B: While future cash flows occurring at the same time can be added directly, the principle applies more broadly to any cash flow stream through present value calculations.
  • C: This describes a balanced cash flow statement, not the additivity principle, which is about valuation consistency.
Question 2 Easy
The no-arbitrage condition in financial markets most likely requires that:
A All investment strategies must have identical returns regardless of risk.
B Equivalent cash flow streams must have equal present values.
C Transaction costs must be zero for all market participants.

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Explanation: The no-arbitrage condition requires that two portfolios or strategies producing identical cash flows in all future states must have the same present value today. If they didn't, investors could buy the cheaper one and sell the expensive one to earn a riskless profit.

Why other options are incorrect:

  • A: Different strategies can have different returns based on their risk profiles. No-arbitrage only requires equivalent strategies to have equal values.
  • C: Transaction costs may exist in practice. The theoretical no-arbitrage condition assumes no costs, but small transaction costs create bounds within which arbitrage isn't profitable.

Question 6 Easy
If two investment strategies have identical cash flows in all future scenarios, the no-arbitrage principle requires that:
A Both strategies must have the same initial cost.
B The strategy with lower risk must have a lower cost.
C The strategies must have identical transaction costs.

‚úì Correct Answer: A

Explanation: If two strategies produce identical future cash flows in all possible scenarios, they must have the same present value (initial cost) today. If one were cheaper, investors could buy the cheaper strategy and sell the expensive one, earning a riskless arbitrage profit.

Key Insight: This is the foundation of derivative pricing and forward rate calculation. By creating replicating portfolios with identical cash flows, we can determine fair prices through the no-arbitrage condition.

Question 8 Medium
Which of the following situations would most likely create a covered interest arbitrage opportunity?
A The forward exchange rate equals the spot rate when domestic and foreign interest rates are equal.
B The forward exchange rate is lower than the rate implied by covered interest parity.
C The currency with the higher interest rate trades at a forward discount.

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Explanation: If the actual forward rate differs from the theoretical rate implied by covered interest parity, an arbitrage opportunity exists. Specifically, if the forward rate is lower than implied, the foreign currency is underpriced on a forward basis.

Arbitrage Strategy:

  1. Borrow domestic currency at domestic rate
  2. Convert to foreign currency at spot rate
  3. Invest in foreign currency at foreign rate
  4. Lock in forward contract to convert back (at the low forward rate)
  5. Repay domestic loan and keep the profit

Why other options are incorrect:

  • A: This describes the correct no-arbitrage condition, not an arbitrage opportunity.
  • C: This is the normal relationship required by covered interest parity, not an arbitrage opportunity.

Question 10 Easy
In the context of forward interest rates, if the yield curve is upward sloping, the forward rate will most likely be:
A Lower than the current short-term rate.
B Higher than the current long-term rate.
C Equal to the average of short-term and long-term rates.

‚úì Correct Answer: B

Explanation: When the yield curve is upward sloping (longer-term rates > shorter-term rates), implied forward rates will be higher than the current long-term spot rate. This is because the forward rate must be high enough to make investors indifferent between locking in the long rate now versus reinvesting at the forward rate later.

Example: If r1 = 3% and r2 = 4%, then:

F1,1 = [(1.04)¬≤ / (1.03)] - 1 ‚âà 5.01%

The forward rate (5.01%) > the 2-year rate (4.00%) > the 1-year rate (3.00%)

Intuition: With an upward-sloping curve, locking in the long rate today gives you a rate between the current short rate and the expected future short rate. For this to be fair, the future rate (forward rate) must be even higher than today's long rate.
